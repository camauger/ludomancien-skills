# Narrative Structure

Framework for creating compelling NPC narrative sections. These transform mechanical stat blocks into characters players remember.

## Core Narrative Sections

### Required for All Significant NPCs (Tier 2+)
1. **Appearance** - What players see
2. **Personality** - How they think and feel
3. **Desire** - What drives them
4. **Secret** - What they hide
5. **Background** - How they got here
6. **Hooks** - How they connect to the plot

### Optional Additions for Major NPCs (Tier 3)
7. **Allure/Presence** - Their distinctive magnetism or impact
8. **A Moment of Vulnerability** - Scene showing their humanity
9. **Multiple Quests** - Interconnected plot threads
10. **Relationships** - Connections to other NPCs
11. **Running [Name]** - GM guidance section

---

## 1. Appearance

**Purpose:** Give GMs and players a clear mental image.

**Structure:**
- Opening impression (1 sentence)
- Physical details (2-3 sentences)
- Distinctive features (1-2 sentences)
- Clothing/presentation (1-2 sentences)
- Closing impression or unique detail (1 sentence)

**What to Include:**
- Age, race/species, build
- Most striking features (eyes, scars, unusual elements)
- How they dress (professional, casual, symbolic)
- Physical presence (intimidating, approachable, forgettable)
- What makes them memorable

**What to Avoid:**
- Exhaustive catalog of every feature
- Generic descriptions ("tall, dark, handsome")
- Details that don't matter ("has ten fingers")
- Contradictory information

**Example (Background NPC):**

> **Marcus Thorne**, fifty-something blacksmith with hammer-scarred 
> hands and a permanent squint from years working the forge. Soot 
> stains never quite wash out of his fingernails. Built like the 
> anvil he beats against—solid, dependable, unmovable. He smells of 
> coal smoke and honest sweat.

**Example (Major NPC):**

> Slender as a knife blade at twenty-five, dark hair falling past her 
> shoulders in waves she never quite tames. Gray-green eyes the color 
> of overcast mornings that hold you with unsettling focus—like she's 
> reading something written beneath your skin.
>
> She dresses in layers of gauzy silk (dove gray, cream, palest blue) 
> that whisper when she moves. Her hands are always ink-stained—
> fingertips and palm edge marked like she's been handling evidence. 
> A silver ring on her right thumb: a gift from her first client.
>
> Nothing about her body screams seduction—but when she touches you 
> with those ink-stained fingers, when she whispers "tell me" against 
> your throat, you'll offer her everything.

## 2. Personality

**Purpose:** Define how the NPC thinks, feels, and makes decisions.

**Core Elements:**

### Dominant Traits (2-3)
Primary characteristics that define their default behavior.

**Examples:**
- "Earnest to the point of seeming naive"
- "Ruthlessly pragmatic, treats people as resources"
- "Gentle but unyielding once principles are involved"
- "Performs confidence while drowning in self-doubt"

### Contradictions
Where they surprise expectations, showing depth.

**Examples:**
- "Violent when necessary but genuinely regrets it"
- "Idealistic about art, cynical about people"
- "Religious but questions every doctrine"
- "Appears cold but actually feels too much"

### Core Conflict
Internal struggle that drives character development.

**Examples:**
- "Torn between duty and conscience"
- "Wants connection, fears vulnerability"
- "Believes in justice, works in corruption"
- "Addicted to empathy that's destroying her"

### Decision-Making Style
How they approach problems and choices.

**Examples:**
- "Analyzes everything, paralyzed by overthinking"
- "Acts on instinct, apologizes later"
- "Consults others, weights consensus"
- "Prays, waits for divine guidance"

**Template:**

> [Name] is **[dominant trait]**, which [manifestation/consequence]. 
> [Their second trait] [creates complexity or contradiction]. 
> 
> **Core Conflict:** [Internal struggle] vs. [opposing force/desire]. 
> This manifests as [behavioral pattern or recurring choice].
>
> **Surprising Depth:** [Something that contradicts surface presentation 
> or reveals hidden dimension].

## 3. Desire

**Purpose:** Define what the NPC wants, providing motivation and hooks.

**Structure:**
- Surface desire (what they claim to want)
- Deep desire (what they actually need)
- How these align or conflict

**Types of Desires:**

### Personal Achievement
- "Prove they belong through understanding"
- "Master a craft or art form"
- "Earn respect they were denied"
- "Become the person they imagine"

### Relational
- "Be truly seen and known"
- "Protect someone they love"
- "Earn forgiveness from estranged family"
- "Find community after exile"

### Ideological
- "Build new theology celebrating embodied joy"
- "Reform corrupt institution from within"
- "Prove a theory or principle"
- "Change cultural values"

### Material/Power
- "Accumulate wealth for security"
- "Gain political influence"
- "Acquire specific artifact or knowledge"
- "Control territory or organization"

**Good Desire Statements:**
- Specific enough to create plot hooks
- Complex enough to create choices
- Achievable but challenging
- Can conflict with other characters' desires

**Example:**

> To prove she belongs in Velnaris not through technique but through 
> *understanding*—to become a courtesan who heals through words and 
> witness, who transforms confession into art so profound that the 
> confessors recognize themselves in her verses and no longer feel 
> alone in their wanting.

## 4. Secret

**Purpose:** Hidden information that creates tension and plot potential.

**Types of Secrets:**

### Identity Secrets
- Using false name/backstory
- Hidden family connections
- Concealed species/magical nature
- Past they're running from

### Relationship Secrets
- Secret affair or romance
- Hidden parentage
- Betrayal of trust
- Forbidden loyalty

### Knowledge Secrets
- Information that could endanger others
- Witnessed a crime
- Discovered a conspiracy
- Knows location of something important

### Action Secrets
- Current espionage or double-agency
- Planning something illegal/dangerous
- Breaking oath or vow
- Addiction or compulsion

### Capability Secrets
- Hidden skills or training
- Magic they don't advertise
- Combat ability they downplay
- Weakness they conceal

**Structure:**

> [The secret itself - 1 sentence]
>
> [How they keep it - 1-2 sentences]
>
> [What happens if revealed - 1-2 sentences]
>
> [Why they haven't revealed it yet - 1 sentence]

**Example:**

> She's an excommunicated priest—her temple cast her out five years 
> ago for preaching that divine love includes erotic love, that bodies 
> are temples meant for pleasure not mortification.
>
> She still wears her priestly vestments, claims continued authority 
> despite excommunication. Most assume she's simply unorthodox, not 
> explicitly condemned.
>
> If the truth becomes widely known, orthodox believers might riot. 
> The church would move from theological opposition to active suppression. 
> Her congregation—built on her claimed legitimacy—might crumble.
>
> She hasn't revealed it because the lie of omission protects everyone 
> who trusts her, and truth might destroy the community she's building.

## 5. Background

**Purpose:** Establish history that explains current behavior and creates connection points.

**Structure:**
- Opening situation (where they started)
- Turning point(s) (what changed them)
- Current status (where they are now)
- Ongoing consequences (how past affects present)

**Length Guidance:**
- Background NPC: 2-3 sentences
- Significant NPC: 2-3 paragraphs
- Major NPC: 3-5 paragraphs + scene examples

**What to Include:**
- Formative experiences
- Key relationships
- Major choices and their consequences
- Skills/knowledge acquisition
- Reputation development

**What to Avoid:**
- Irrelevant childhood details
- Every job they've ever held
- Complete family tree
- Day-by-day accounting

**Example (Significant NPC):**

> Magistrate Vorn rose through the bureaucracy through competence and 
> compromise—he truly believed in justice once, but twenty years of 
> watching the wealthy buy verdicts corroded that idealism into cynicism.
>
> The bribes started small: looking the other way on minor infractions, 
> ensuring certain cases landed on lenient judges' desks. He told himself 
> he was just working within the system, being pragmatic.
>
> Then his daughter got sick. The medicine she needed cost more than ten 
> years of honest salary. He took a larger bribe—ruled against an innocent 
> woman who was later executed. His daughter died anyway. The guilt has 
> been eating him alive ever since, but he can't stop taking bribes now—
> the debts and dependencies have become too complex.

## 6. Hooks

**Purpose:** Create immediate ways for PCs to interact with the NPC and engage with their story.

**Types:**

### If Approached
How NPC responds to player initiation. What service, information, or interaction they offer.

### If Pressed
What players can extract with effort—favors, information, special access.

### If Refused
What happens if players decline the NPC's offer or initial hook.

### If In Immediate Danger
Crisis situation that demands immediate PC intervention or decision.

**Structure for Each Hook:**

> ### [Hook Type]
> 
> [Immediate situation - what's happening now]
>
> [What the NPC wants/needs from PCs]
>
> [Consequences of action/inaction]
>
> [Mechanical benefits if appropriate]

**Example Set:**

> ### If Approached
> She begins with tea and questions that feel like caresses. When 
> confession breaks into physical need—when your words run out and only 
> touch remains—her mouth finds yours with the same careful attention 
> she gave your secrets.
>
> ### If Pressed
> She's been collecting confessions for three years now—hundreds of 
> secrets. If you've earned her trust, she might share patterns she's 
> observed: connections between seemingly unrelated confessions that 
> point to larger conspiracies.
>
> ### If Refused
> After you decline, you find a poem slipped under your door. It 
> describes something you've never told anyone—a fear, a regret, a 
> desire you've hidden. She saw it somehow, and the verses acknowledge: 
> *I witness this truth you carry, and you are not alone in it.*
>
> ### If In Immediate Danger
> You find her locked in her room, three days gone, drowning in someone 
> else's grief, boundaries so dissolved she doesn't recognize you. She 
> needs to be brought back—and someone outside is listening, waiting 
> to discover what's happened to the empath who knows too many secrets.

## 7. Allure/Presence (Optional - For Specific Roles)

**Purpose:** Describe the distinctive magnetism or impact this NPC has on others.

**Use For:**
- Courtesans, seducers, manipulators
- Charismatic leaders
- Intimidating authority figures
- Anyone whose personal presence is plot-relevant

**Structure:**
- What draws people (1-2 sentences)
- How it manifests physically (1-2 sentences)
- Psychological/emotional effect (2-3 sentences)
- What people say about them (1 sentence)

**Example:**

> Cirelle listens like she's memorizing the geography of your soul—
> wide-eyed and utterly present, as if your confession is scripture 
> she'll treasure forever. Her skin carries parchment and jasmine tea. 
> Her touch isn't practiced seduction but genuine curiosity, as if 
> your body is a text she's learning to read.
>
> Clients leave feeling strangely lighter, as if she took their shame 
> and alchemized it into something beautiful enough to bear. Some say 
> making love to Cirelle feels like being transformed into poetry.

## 8. A Moment of Vulnerability (Optional - Major NPCs)

**Purpose:** Provide a scene where the NPC's defenses are down, revealing their humanity.

**Structure:**
- Setting (where/when players find them)
- Immediate state (what they're doing/feeling)
- What breaks their composure
- What they reveal
- How they recover (or don't)
- What this moment means for relationship with PCs

**Length:** 3-6 paragraphs

**Example Opening:**

> You find Solace in her chapel, on her knees before the altar, crying. 
> Not quiet weeping—sobbing that shakes her entire body, gasping between 
> breaths like someone drowning. Her vestments are rumpled, twisted from 
> kneeling hunched over.
>
> "I received a letter," she manages, voice rough. "From my former temple. 
> They're holding a formal ceremony of condemnation..."

## 9. Multiple Quests (Optional - Major NPCs)

**Purpose:** Provide interconnected plot threads at different scales.

**Structure Per Quest:**

> ### [Quest Title]
>
> [Opener - the core situation in 1-2 sentences]
>
> **The Stakes:** [What matters, who's affected]
>
> **The Complication:** [Why it's not simple]
>
> **The Crisis:** [Immediate trigger for PC involvement]
>
> **Hook Options:** [3-5 different entry points]

**Quest Types:**

### Personal Quest
Resolves NPC's internal conflict or achieves their desire

### Relational Quest
Involves their relationships with other NPCs

### Factional Quest
Connects to larger organizations or power structures

### Philosophical Quest
Explores themes and ideas embodied by the NPC

**Example:**

> ### The Poem
>
> Her manuscript contains three hundred pages of confessions disguised 
> as poetry—the most complete map of Velnaris's hidden soul ever created.
>
> **The Stakes:** If published, every powerful figure she's documented 
> faces ruin. The House's reputation crumbles. The covenant of discretion 
> is destroyed.
>
> **The Complication:** The manuscript is the only place her empathy 
> goes that isn't inside her body. Destroy it, and she drowns in 
> vicarious suffering.
>
> **The Crisis:** A powerful client recognized his confession in 
> circulating verses. He's hired someone to retrieve the manuscript 
> in three days.
>
> **Hook Options:**
> - Hired to steal it
> - Hired to protect it
> - Help her decide its fate
> - Caught between factions

## Common Mistakes

**Too Much Information:** Not every NPC needs 5000 words. Match detail to importance.

**No Clear Desire:** "Wants to be happy" isn't specific enough. What would happiness look like?

**Unconnected Elements:** Every detail should either reveal character or create plot hooks.

**Perfect People:** Flaws, contradictions, and mistakes make NPCs relatable.

**No Clear Voice:** If you can't imagine how they'd say something, neither can the GM.

**Secrets That Don't Matter:** If revealing the secret wouldn't change anything, it's not a secret—it's trivia.

## Quick Templates

### The Quest Giver
- **Appearance:** Professional but stressed
- **Personality:** Desperate but competent
- **Desire:** Solve specific problem
- **Secret:** Withholding key information
- **Hook:** Offers payment for help

### The Rival
- **Appearance:** Similar to PC but twisted
- **Personality:** Competent and competitive
- **Desire:** Prove superiority
- **Secret:** Hiding vulnerability
- **Hook:** Contests same goal

### The Mentor
- **Appearance:** Aged, scarred, wise
- **Personality:** Patient but demanding
- **Desire:** Pass on legacy
- **Secret:** Past failure haunts them
- **Hook:** Sees potential in PCs

### The Love Interest
- **Appearance:** Attractive in their terms
- **Personality:** Complementary to PC
- **Desire:** Connection or specific goal
- **Secret:** Incompatible commitment
- **Hook:** Genuine chemistry + obstacle

### The Innocent
- **Appearance:** Young or naive markers
- **Personality:** Trusting, hopeful
- **Desire:** Survive with values intact
- **Secret:** Saw something dangerous
- **Hook:** Needs protection from threat
