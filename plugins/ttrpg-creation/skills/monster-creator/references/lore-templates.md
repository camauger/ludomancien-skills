# Lore and Ecology Templates

Eleven category-specific templates for the Ecology section of a
monster entry. Each template provides the *narrative shape* of the
creature's existence: where it lives, what it does, how it relates
to the world.

A monster's ecology category is usually independent of its CR or
type. A CR 1 wolf and a CR 15 owlbear can both be Apex Predators; a
CR 5 goblin and a CR 20 demon lord can both be Sentient Collectives.

---

## 1. Apex Predator

A solitary or near-solitary hunter at the top of its local food
chain. Acts on hunger and territory.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A natural species or a specifically evolved
  hunter. Often the dominant predator of a biome.
- **Habitat:** Defined territory of [N] square miles. Marks
  boundaries with kill sites, scent, sound, or visible signs.
- **Behavior:** Solitary (except mating, raising young, or in lean
  seasons). Hunts at [time of day] using [strategy: ambush / stalking
  / pursuit / cooperative]. Hunting range encompasses [territory
  description].
- **Diet:** [Primary prey], occasionally [secondary prey]. Will avoid
  [feared prey] if alternatives exist.
- **Signs of Presence:** Territorial markings, dismembered prey,
  characteristic sounds at [time], displaced wildlife, the absence
  of smaller animals in its hunting range.
- **Relationships:** Apex predators usually have **no peers**, only
  prey and competitors. Competing predators within the territory
  are driven out or killed. Smaller scavengers follow the predator
  for leftover meat.
- **Treasure:** Whatever its prey carried. Usually rotting equipment,
  bones, occasional metal items in scat. Rare valuables only at the
  predator's most-used kill site.

### Example creatures

- Bulette (territorial burrower)
- Owlbear (forest apex)
- Manticore (mountain apex)
- Adult Black Dragon (swamp apex)
- Roper (cave apex)
- Mountain Lion (mundane natural)

### Design notes

- Apex Predators rarely fight to the death. They flee at 25–40% HP.
- Their threat is **strategic** (you can't safely cross their
  territory) more than **tactical** (winning the fight against one
  is achievable; finding it again later is the problem).
- Plot hooks naturally involve: bounty hunting, prey-clearing for a
  village, ecological investigation, mating-season aggression.

## 2. Ambush Hunter

A predator that wins by *not being seen* until it strikes. Trades
sustained combat for burst damage. Usually solitary.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A species evolved (or magically twisted) for
  ambush. Crypsis, camouflage, or environmental mimicry is
  fundamental.
- **Habitat:** Dense or visually complex environments — forest
  understorey, kelp beds, ceiling crevices, jungle canopy, sand
  dunes, urban rooftops, sewer networks.
- **Behavior:** Establishes ambush points (multiple per territory)
  and rotates between them. Returns to a successful site less often
  than to a failed one (avoids being patterned).
- **Diet:** Single large prey items rather than multiple small.
  Eats slowly, often returns to the kill over several days.
- **Signs of Presence:** Subtle. Disturbed leaf litter at established
  perches, prey-bones in caches the hunter returns to, characteristic
  scratch marks at hiding spots, prey animals giving certain areas
  a wide berth.
- **Relationships:** Avoids confrontation with apex predators in the
  same territory; shares space with smaller, non-competing species.
  Rarely cooperative.
- **Treasure:** Items prey was carrying, accumulated at cache sites.
  Often surprisingly rich at a long-used cache.

### Example creatures

- Giant Spider (web-based ambush)
- Mimic (object camouflage)
- Slithering Tracker (stealth + grappler)
- Ankheg (subterranean ambush)
- Veiled Stalker (shadow ambush)

### Design notes

- Ambush Hunters live and die by stealth. A skilled party with high
  passive Perception trivializes them; a careless party gets
  surprised and burst-damaged.
- Plot hooks: villagers disappearing without trace, livestock losses
  with no predator tracks, ranger-tracker investigations.

## 3. Swarm

A collective creature comprising many small individuals functioning
as one. Treated as a single combatant with swarm mechanics.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A species whose individual members are small,
  but whose collective behavior emerges into something more
  threatening. May be natural (rats, bats, locusts) or magical
  (animated bones, cursed insects).
- **Habitat:** Wherever the constituent species can live in numbers.
  Often: damp dark places (rats), caves (bats), corpse-rich areas
  (insects), wherever the source can multiply.
- **Behavior:** Collective behaviors emerge from individual reactions
  to stimuli. Doesn't plan; reacts en masse. Triggered by food
  scent, perceived threat, or magical compulsion.
- **Diet:** What the individual creatures eat, multiplied. Often
  carrion, blood, dropped food, vegetation. Some swarms (insect
  swarms) consume living tissue.
- **Signs of Presence:** Smaller signs of the individual creatures
  (droppings, exoskeletons, distinctive sounds), but at vast
  amplitude. A normal-rat-sized creature leaves rat-sign; a
  rat-swarm leaves a *village's worth* of rat-sign.
- **Relationships:** Predators of the swarm species cull individuals
  but rarely affect the swarm itself. The swarm may protect or
  serve a controlling intelligence (necromancer with rat-swarm,
  druid with insect-swarm).
- **Treasure:** Whatever has been dragged into the swarm's nesting
  area. Usually small valuables — coins, rings, gemstones.

### Example creatures

- Swarm of Rats
- Swarm of Bats
- Swarm of Insects
- Swarm of Quippers (aquatic)
- Crawling Claws (severed hands)
- Skeletons (animated bones) operating as a swarm

### Design notes

- Swarms have resistance to many damage types (because individual
  members survive area attacks while the swarm continues). This is
  mechanical, not flavor.
- Plot hooks: pest control gone wrong, necromantic experiments,
  ecological collapse, vermin tied to a specific cult.

## 4. Scavenger

A creature that survives by exploiting the kills and corpses of
others. Less aggressive than predators but more dangerous than they
appear when threatened or when their food source is challenged.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** Evolved to clean up after predators, or a
  species adapted to occupy a corpse-rich ecological niche. Some
  are aware enough to *create* the carcasses they feed on.
- **Habitat:** Battlefields, charnel pits, dungeon corridors,
  graveyards, abandoned settlements. Wherever death accumulates.
- **Behavior:** Follows larger predators or established
  death-producing forces. Cleans up carrion within hours of death.
  Will defend a fresh kill but rarely attacks healthy prey.
- **Diet:** Carrion, decomposing flesh, sometimes bones (some
  scavengers crush bones for marrow). May supplement with small
  living prey.
- **Signs of Presence:** Bones stripped clean, often left in a
  characteristic pile or scatter pattern. Drag marks from carcasses
  being moved to a nest or den. Distinct smell.
- **Relationships:** Tolerated by apex predators (cleaning service);
  feared by herbivores and prey species; sometimes preyed upon by
  larger scavengers.
- **Treasure:** Bones, sometimes valuable metal items pulled from
  prey (jewelry from corpses, coins from pockets).

### Example creatures

- Ghouls (intelligent scavengers, undead variant)
- Gnolls (organized scavenger-raiders)
- Vultures and Giant Vultures
- Hyenas and Hyena-derived

### Design notes

- Scavengers exist in *ecological dependence* on predators or
  battlefields. Their presence often signals the presence of
  something bigger nearby.
- Plot hooks: investigating a battlefield aftermath, tracking gnoll
  warbands to their corpse-dragging trails, encountering ghouls in
  catacombs, scavengers leading the party to undiscovered ruins.

## 5. Parasite

A creature that lives in, on, or off another creature, often
beneath the host's awareness. Reproductive cycle ties to hosts.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A species evolved (or sorcerously created)
  to exploit hosts. Smaller than its hosts; survival depends on
  evading detection or overpowering immune responses.
- **Habitat:** Wherever hosts exist. Inside hosts' bodies,
  burrowed into skin, attached externally, nesting in
  blood-rich areas. Some parasites live in specific environments
  that hosts inhabit (water, caves, forests) and wait for hosts.
- **Behavior:** Reproductive cycle is the parasite's primary
  driver. Lays eggs in hosts, releases spores, attaches and
  feeds. Often inactive or dormant outside of hosts.
- **Diet:** Host's blood, flesh, fluids, or the host's
  recently-consumed food. Some parasites consume host's mental
  energy (psychic parasites).
- **Signs of Presence:** Symptoms in hosts (wasting, mental
  disturbance, unusual marks, characteristic illness). Empty
  husks of past hosts. Host species absent from areas where
  parasite levels are high. Local healers report a "new
  sickness".
- **Relationships:** Defined entirely by the host species. Multiple
  parasites can coexist in one host (multi-parasite ecosystems).
- **Treasure:** None to speak of — parasites accumulate nothing.

### Example creatures

- Stirge (blood parasite)
- Intellect Devourer (brain parasite)
- Mind Flayer's tadpole (ceremorphosis)
- Various magical infestations (cursed by a hag, etc.)

### Design notes

- Parasites work best when the *parasite is the antagonist of the
  story*, not just an encounter. The party often has to find the
  parasite *inside* an NPC or location.
- Plot hooks: cure-the-sickness arcs, identifying the parasite's
  reproductive cycle, finding the original source, body horror.

## 6. Sentient Collective

A culture, society, or hive-minded species of intelligent beings.
Treats the species *as a whole* through its representative member.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A people with culture, history, and self-
  awareness. Speaks language(s). Has goals beyond eating and
  reproduction.
- **Habitat:** Settlements (villages, cities, fortresses, hidden
  enclaves). Adapted to specific climates or biomes; may have
  expanded beyond.
- **Behavior:** Cooperative within the group, often hostile or
  competitive toward outgroups. Social structure (hierarchy,
  hierarchy of clans, hive mind). Cultural practices that may
  appear strange or alien.
- **Diet:** Cultivated food, hunted game, traded goods, sometimes
  ritual cannibalism (rare, dark). Most sentient collectives have
  diverse diets due to trade.
- **Signs of Presence:** Settlements, roads, trade goods,
  characteristic architecture, languages overheard, religious or
  cultural markers.
- **Relationships:** Diplomatic relationships with other sentient
  collectives. Trade networks. Long-running feuds or alliances.
- **Treasure:** Cultural artifacts, traded valuables, religious
  items, accumulated wealth in settlements.

### Example creatures

- Goblins (clan-based)
- Hobgoblins (military structure)
- Drow (matriarchal city-state)
- Mind Flayers (hive-mind)
- Modrons (rigid hierarchy)
- Yuan-ti (snake-cult city-states)

### Design notes

- Sentient Collectives are *cultures*, not just enemies. They have
  internal divisions, dissidents, refugees, exiles, and reformers.
- A stat block here represents a *typical member*; named individuals
  (chieftains, priests) are NPCs built via `npc-creator` on top of
  this stat block.
- Plot hooks: diplomatic mission, infiltration of a settlement,
  internal faction conflict, exile NPC seeking help, ancient
  treaty broken.

## 7. Undead Echo

A creature animated from death — either by trauma (ghost), by
magic (skeleton, zombie), or by the creature's own driving will
(lich, vampire). The "echo" framing emphasizes that the creature is
tied to *something* from its prior existence.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** Specifically, *how* it became undead. Murdered
  by betrayal? Died in childbirth and the trauma echoed? Volunteered
  for liches' bargain? Mass-grave under a battle curse?
- **Habitat:** The site of death OR a site significant to the prior
  life. Wraiths haunt where they died; vampires often inhabit their
  ancestral homes; ghosts cling to a specific place or object;
  liches build phylactery-protected fortresses.
- **Behavior:** Driven by the unfinished business of the prior life.
  Often pursues a specific goal (revenge, completion, protection of
  a loved one, preservation of self) and ignores or destroys
  whatever interferes.
- **Diet:** Sometimes blood (vampire), sometimes life essence
  (wraith, specter), sometimes nothing biological at all (lich,
  ghost). The need to "feed" reflects the creature's unfinished
  business.
- **Signs of Presence:** Cold spots, dimming lights, environmental
  decay, the absence of small animals, characteristic sounds
  (footsteps where no one walks, distant weeping, scratching at
  doors). For powerful undead, regional effects (see Section 8 of
  `trait-and-action-library.md`).
- **Relationships:** Tied to the prior life. Loved ones, enemies,
  unfinished obligations. Sometimes allied with other undead
  through necromantic creator.
- **Treasure:** What the prior life accumulated. Vampires hold
  estates; liches hoard knowledge and magical items; ghosts
  preserve sentimental items; banshees keep nothing.

### Example creatures

- Ghost (specific haunting)
- Wraith (echo of violent death)
- Banshee (mourning echo)
- Vampire (sustained intelligence after death)
- Lich (deliberate self-transformation)
- Death Knight (bound to a fallen vow)
- Mummy (entombed echo with curses)

### Design notes

- Undead Echoes work best with **a specific story**. A generic ghost
  is forgettable; the ghost of *the betrayed bride who haunts the
  manor where her husband's portrait still hangs* is memorable.
- Often the creature can be put to rest through narrative resolution
  rather than combat (or in addition to it).
- Plot hooks: investigating the prior life, resolving unfinished
  business, retrieving sentimental items, breaking the curse that
  binds the creature.

## 8. Fiendish Bargain

A creature that exists through a bargain — its own or another's.
Demons and devils typically appear in the world only through
specific summoning, infernal contracts, or planar incursions.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A native of the lower planes (Abyss, Hells,
  Hades, etc.). Tied to a specific role in infernal hierarchy or
  Abyssal chaos. May be summoned to the material plane via specific
  bargains, rituals, or breaches.
- **Habitat:** On the material plane, wherever a summoner or bargain
  brought it. In its native plane, distinct planar geography (City
  of Brass for efreet, layers of the Hells for devils, infinite
  layers of the Abyss for demons).
- **Behavior:** Driven by the bargain's terms (devils) or by
  chaotic appetite (demons). Pursues the goal of whoever summoned
  it OR escapes the bargain to pursue its own goals OR subverts
  the bargain through technicality.
- **Diet:** Souls (devils), suffering (demons), faith and worship
  (some fiends), nothing biological. The "feeding" is metaphysical.
- **Signs of Presence:** Sulfurous smell, unusual hot or cold spots,
  shadows that move wrong, animals refusing to enter the area,
  reports of voices, blasphemous symbols appearing in places they
  shouldn't.
- **Relationships:** Defined by the bargain — to a summoner, to a
  patron, to a victim, to a rival fiend. Fiend factions politic
  endlessly.
- **Treasure:** Often valuable but cursed. Coins from previous
  victims, infernal coinage that fades after exit, talismans that
  bind or bind back.

### Example creatures

- Imp (familiar fiend)
- Quasit (lesser demon)
- Bearded Devil, Erinyes, Horned Devil, Pit Fiend (infernal
  hierarchy)
- Vrock, Hezrou, Glabrezu, Balor (demonic chaos)
- Night Hag (Hag from the Hells variant)
- Rakshasa (reincarnating fiend)

### Design notes

- Fiendish Bargain creatures work best when **the bargain is the
  plot**. The party doesn't just fight the demon — they untangle
  who summoned it, why, what they offered, and whether the bargain
  can be broken.
- Mortal summoners are usually more dangerous than the fiend
  itself; the party's real opponent is human.
- Plot hooks: identifying the summoner, breaking the bargain,
  fulfilling the bargain on someone else's behalf, negotiating with
  the fiend.

## 9. Fey Trickster

A creature of the Feywild or a magical-nature force. Operates by
rules other than mortal logic — bargains, riddles, transformations,
glamour.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A being of the Feywild, or a manifestation
  of natural magic that touches mortal reality. Tied to specific
  natural elements (a particular forest, a single stream, the dusk
  hour).
- **Habitat:** Liminal spaces — woodland glades, crossroads, mist-
  filled valleys, ancient trees, places where the Feywild touches
  the material plane.
- **Behavior:** Driven by Fey logic: pranks, bargains, jealousies,
  feasts, oaths. Rarely fight to the death; usually bargain,
  trick, or flee. Take offense at perceived rudeness; reward
  perceived kindness.
- **Diet:** Often metaphysical — songs, emotions, dreams. Sometimes
  food (cream and honey for some Fey), sometimes nothing.
- **Signs of Presence:** Mushroom rings, dropped silver bells, lost
  time (the party emerges to find a day passed), animals acting
  strangely (cats grinning, birds speaking single words), nighttime
  music with no source.
- **Relationships:** Hierarchies of Fey courts (Seelie, Unseelie),
  individual jealousies, ancient grudges with iron-wielders, pacts
  with mortal druids or warlocks.
- **Treasure:** Beautiful but impractical — silver dust, dewdrops
  in glass vials, woven flower-crowns, songs bound in seed pods.
  Sometimes pacts that are "treasures" if redeemed correctly,
  curses if not.

### Example creatures

- Pixie, Sprite, Brownie
- Dryad, Satyr
- Hag (Green, Night, Sea — Hags are Fey-aligned)
- Faerie Dragon
- Redcap (Unseelie)
- Quickling
- Centaur (varies by edition; sometimes Fey, sometimes Monstrosity)

### Design notes

- Fey Tricksters thrive in **non-combat encounters**. A bargain, a
  riddle, a deception is often more memorable than a fight.
- Iron weapons, religious symbols (sometimes), and "true names"
  often have power over Fey.
- Plot hooks: pact-broken, kidnapped-by-fey, lost-time-recovery,
  bargain-bartering, sister-stolen-and-replaced-by-changeling.

## 10. Elemental Embodiment

A creature made of pure elemental matter — fire, water, earth, air,
or more exotic elements (smoke, magma, ice, lightning). Driven by
elemental impulse rather than thought.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** Native to the elemental planes (Plane of
  Fire, Water, Earth, Air). Manifests on the material plane through
  natural elemental abundance OR through summoning.
- **Habitat:** Wherever the corresponding element is abundant —
  fire elementals near volcanoes or massive fires, water elementals
  in deep oceans, earth elementals in mountains and underground,
  air elementals in storm fronts.
- **Behavior:** Embodied impulse, not cunning. Fire elementals
  consume fuel; water elementals follow currents; earth elementals
  pursue paths of least resistance through stone. Don't have
  agendas; have *natures*.
- **Diet:** Their element. Fire elementals burn fuel; water
  elementals do not need to "eat" but flow toward sources; earth
  elementals are sustained by stone itself.
- **Signs of Presence:** Localized weather, unusual elemental
  concentration (a too-hot region, an underwater current that
  shouldn't exist, a calm zone in a storm). Trail-of-fire or
  trail-of-water where the elemental passed.
- **Relationships:** Elementals have favored elements and feared
  elements. Fire fears water; water dissolves earth; air scatters
  flame. Some elementals bind into compounds (magma = fire+earth,
  ice = water+air, etc.).
- **Treasure:** Rarely accumulates treasure. May be near sites of
  elemental concentration (gems near earth elementals, glass near
  fire elementals).

### Example creatures

- Air Elemental, Fire Elemental, Water Elemental, Earth Elemental
- Magmin (small magma)
- Salamander (intelligent fire being)
- Galeb Duhr (rolling stone elemental)
- Ice Mephit, Magma Mephit, Smoke Mephit (lesser combined elementals)
- Djinni, Efreeti, Marid, Dao (Greater Elemental beings, more
  sentient)

### Design notes

- Elemental Embodiments are best when the **environment is also the
  element**. A fire elemental in a volcanic cavern; a water
  elemental in flooding caves; an air elemental atop a windswept
  peak.
- Their resistances/immunities make them difficult to fight with the
  "wrong" damage type. Forces party to adapt.
- Plot hooks: contained-elemental-released, summoned-and-banished
  scenarios, environmental imbalance traced to elemental
  concentration.

## 11. Planar Exile

A creature from a specific plane (Shadowfell, Astral, Ethereal,
Mechanus, etc.) that has been displaced or fled to the material
plane and now operates within unfamiliar rules.

### Ecology structure

- **Origin / Nature:** A specific plane and a specific reason for
  leaving (exile, escape, hunt, mission, accident). The plane of
  origin shapes the creature's nature.
- **Habitat:** A place on the material plane that *partially
  mirrors* the plane of origin. Shadowfell-exiled creatures favor
  perpetual shadow; Astral-exiled creatures favor stillness and
  high places; Plane-of-Mirrors exiles favor reflection-rich
  environments.
- **Behavior:** Adapted to its home plane's logic; alien to material
  logic. May pursue the goal that exiled it (return, revenge,
  fulfill a mission) OR adapt to the new plane and become a local
  threat.
- **Diet:** Whatever it ate at home, OR a material-plane substitute
  that approximates it.
- **Signs of Presence:** Environmental anomalies that hint at the
  plane of origin (shadows that don't match their casters, gravity
  shifts, temperature swings, time anomalies, mirror-images that
  move).
- **Relationships:** May have planar contacts (other exiles, beings
  of the same plane who occasionally manifest). May be hunted by
  beings from its home plane.
- **Treasure:** Items from the plane of origin (shadowsilk cloak,
  star-iron blade, mirror-glass shards). Often valuable to
  spellcasters and planar scholars.

### Example creatures

- Shadow Demon (Shadowfell exile)
- Astral Stalker, Githyanki (Astral plane operators)
- Phase Spider (Ethereal plane crosser)
- Slaad (Limbo exile)
- Bodak (death-plane exile, Negative Energy Plane)

### Design notes

- Planar Exiles raise the campaign's stakes. They imply the
  existence of other planes and the possibility of inter-planar
  travel.
- Their environmental anomalies are *clues* — a clever party can
  trace the planar signature back to the creature.
- Plot hooks: investigating planar anomalies, banishing the
  exile back to its plane, helping the exile complete its mission,
  exploiting the exile for planar travel.

## 12. Selecting a Template

When designing a new monster, match its concept to one (or two)
templates:

| Concept clue | Most likely template |
|---|---|
| "Solo hunter of [biome]" | Apex Predator |
| "Hides and strikes from cover" | Ambush Hunter |
| "Lots of small individuals as one threat" | Swarm |
| "Lives off carrion / battlefield" | Scavenger |
| "Lives in or on a host" | Parasite |
| "Has language, culture, history" | Sentient Collective |
| "Came back from death with purpose" | Undead Echo |
| "Summoned through bargain" | Fiendish Bargain |
| "Lives in liminal spaces, bargains in riddles" | Fey Trickster |
| "Made of pure [element]" | Elemental Embodiment |
| "Doesn't belong on this plane" | Planar Exile |

Mixed concepts (an undead apex predator, a Fey scavenger, a
sentient parasite) are valid — use two templates and blend the
elements.
