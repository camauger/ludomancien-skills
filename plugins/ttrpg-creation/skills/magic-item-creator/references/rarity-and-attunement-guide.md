# Rarity and Attunement Guide — D&D 5e 2024

The five rarity tiers with their power-level expectations, gold price
ranges, reward cadence by campaign tier, and attunement rules.

**Honest note on sources:** the 2024 Dungeon Master's Guide includes a
revised magic item chapter with adjusted pricing and a refined approach
to reward distribution. The benchmarks below derive from the 2014 DMG
table (Magic Item Pricing, Magic Item Rewards) which the 2024 version
preserves with light adjustments. Use as **design targets** — verify
against the 2024 DMG before publishing if the product targets official
tournament or commercial play.

---

## 1. The Five Rarity Tiers

| Rarity | Power level | Typical owner | Discovery context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common | Cosmetic / utility | Apprentice mage, village priest | Found in dungeons, sold in cities |
| Uncommon | Modest combat or utility advantage | Adventuring group, low-tier antagonist | Treasures of mid-CR encounters |
| Rare | Notable combat or utility advantage | Veteran adventurer, regional faction leader | Rewards from significant quests |
| Very Rare | Powerful, identity-defining | Heroic adventurer, major NPC | Hard-won prize, sealed in a tomb |
| Legendary | Campaign-defining, unique-feeling | Capstone heroes, archvillains, deity-touched | Climactic discovery, final reward |

## 2. Gold Price Ranges

The DMG provides recommended gold value ranges by rarity. Prices vary
considerably by region, demand, and whether the item is being bought
from a vendor (markup) or sold to one (50% of value).

| Rarity | Price range (gp, mundane economy) | Price range (in scarce-magic settings) |
|---|---|---|
| Common | 50–100 | 100–250 |
| Uncommon | 101–500 | 400–1,000 |
| Rare | 501–5,000 | 2,500–8,000 |
| Very Rare | 5,001–50,000 | 20,000–60,000 |
| Legendary | 50,001+ | Often "not for sale" |

**Use cases:**
- *Sell to player at vendor:* upper end of range × markup
- *Player sells to vendor:* lower end of range × 0.5
- *Black market / underground:* sometimes 2× upper end for restricted items
- *Found as treasure:* range applies to "if appraised"

**Important caveat:** these prices are *suggestions*. Many tables run
"low magic" settings where prices are inflated 3–10× to reflect
scarcity. Other tables run "high magic" with these prices as floors.

### Setting-specific considerations

- **Forgotten Realms (default):** standard pricing applies
- **Dark Sun / low-magic frontier:** ×3 to ×10 the standard range
- **Eberron (magic-as-industry):** common items are vendor-stocked,
  uncommon items are guild-grade, rare items still rare
- **Custom dark fantasy setting:** typically scarce-magic pricing
  unless specified otherwise

## 3. Reward Cadence by Campaign Tier

The 2014 DMG suggests how many magic items a character should
accumulate across a campaign. The 2024 DMG preserves this overall
shape with minor tweaks. These are **suggestions**, not rules.

| Tier | Character levels | Total items per character (suggested) | Of which legendary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | Levels 1–4 | 0–3 | 0 |
| Tier 2 | Levels 5–10 | 3–6 | 0–1 |
| Tier 3 | Levels 11–16 | 6–9 | 1–2 |
| Tier 4 | Levels 17–20 | 9–12 | 2–3 |

### Reading the cadence

Across Tiers 1–4, a character might end the campaign with ~10–12
items: roughly 2–3 Common, 3–4 Uncommon, 2–3 Rare, 1–2 Very Rare,
and 1–2 Legendary. This includes consumables (potions, scrolls)
which are short-lived.

### Reward composition by tier

| Tier | Common | Uncommon | Rare | Very Rare | Legendary |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Common, mostly utility | One Uncommon by level 3–4 | — | — | — |
| 2 | Refresh Common | Multiple Uncommon | One Rare by level 8–10 | — | — |
| 3 | Refresh Common, Uncommon | More Uncommon | Multiple Rare | One Very Rare | — |
| 4 | — | — | More Rare | Multiple Very Rare | One Legendary capstone |

### Pace implications for design

When designing a single magic item, ask: *will this be the player's only
item at this rarity for several levels?* If yes, the item carries the
weight of identity. If it's the third Rare in a swap-trinket campaign,
it can lean more utility-focused.

## 4. Attunement Rules

Attunement is the 5e mechanism that controls magic item power.

### Core attunement rules (2024)

- **Maximum attuned items per character: 3** (some classes / features
  expand to 4)
- **Attunement requires a Short Rest** of focus on the item
- **Attunement persists** until the character dies, breaks attunement
  voluntarily, attunes to a different item that requires a slot, or
  is forcibly separated (varies by setting)
- **Most items requiring attunement** specify what *type* of character
  can attune: spellcaster of any tradition, paladin, druid, etc.

### When to require attunement

| Item type | Attunement? |
|---|---|
| Common, simple utility | Usually no |
| Common cosmetic / flavor | No |
| Uncommon, no combat impact | No or yes |
| Uncommon, combat impact | Usually yes |
| Rare, combat or major utility | Usually yes |
| Very Rare | Yes (almost always) |
| Legendary | Yes (with prerequisite) |
| Cursed flavor (no mechanical drawback) | Yes |
| Sentient (even minor) | Yes |

### Attunement prerequisites

Adding a prerequisite to attunement sharpens the item's identity:

| Prerequisite | Effect |
|---|---|
| Spellcaster of any class | "Mage's tool" type items |
| Spellcaster of [specific class] | Class-flavored items (Paladin's relic, Cleric's holy weapon) |
| Specific alignment | Faith-bound items, devotional artifacts |
| Specific ancestry / lineage | Heritage items, ancestral bloodline keys |
| Minimum ability score (e.g., Strength 17) | Items that require the wielder's might |
| Specific class feature | "Channel Divinity user", "Wild Shape user" |
| Specific deity / patron worship | Cleric/Warlock-bound items |
| Specific level requirement | "Only attunes for characters of level 11 or higher" |

### Attunement prerequisites by rarity (typical patterns)

| Rarity | Typical prereq pattern |
|---|---|
| Common | None |
| Uncommon | Class-specific OR none |
| Rare | Class OR ability OR alignment specific |
| Very Rare | Compound prereq (e.g., "Paladin of a good alignment with Cha 17") |
| Legendary | Compound + narrative (e.g., "must complete a specific oath ritual") |

### Multiple-attuner items

Some Legendary and Artifact items allow only one attuner *in
existence at a time* — if attuned, no one else can attune to it
even if the current attuner dies. This is a powerful identity
device for Legendary items.

### Voluntary breaking

A character can break attunement by spending a Short Rest. Some
items have hooks for forced or punishing breaking ("If the item
leaves the wielder's possession for more than 24 hours, they must
make a DC X Charisma save or lose attunement until they reclaim
it").

## 5. Slot Rules for Wondrous Items

Wondrous Items are the catch-all category — anything that isn't an
Armor, Weapon, Potion, Ring, Rod, Scroll, Staff, or Wand. They
include cloaks, boots, helms, gloves, amulets, rings of holding,
etc.

The 5e system does not enforce strict slot rules (you can wear two
Cloaks of Elvenkind, mechanically). But narratively and as a design
discipline, most tables observe rough body-slot conventions:

| Slot | Common items in this slot |
|---|---|
| Head | Helm of Brilliance, Circlet of Blasting, Hat of Disguise |
| Eyes | Eyes of Charming, Eyes of the Eagle, Goggles of Night |
| Neck | Amulet of Health, Amulet of Proof against Detection, Periapt |
| Shoulders | Cloak of Elvenkind, Cloak of Protection, Cloak of Displacement |
| Body / chest | Robe of the Archmagi, Vestments, Mantle of X |
| Arms | Bracers of Defense, Bracers of Archery |
| Hands | Gauntlets of Ogre Power, Gloves of Missile Snaring, Gloves of Thievery |
| Waist | Belt of Giant Strength (any), Belt of Dwarvenkind |
| Feet | Boots of Speed, Boots of Striding and Springing, Winged Boots |
| Finger (max 2) | Ring of Protection, Ring of Spell Storing, Ring of Resistance |
| Held | Wands, Staves, Rods (these use Item-Slot Rod/Wand/Staff) |
| Slotless / pocket | Bag of Holding, Decanter of Endless Water, Marvelous Pigments |

### Slot design discipline

When designing a Wondrous Item:

1. Pick a slot that matches the item's flavor (a helm sits on the
   head, a cloak on the shoulders).
2. Avoid stacking 2+ effects on the same slot (a "Cloak of Elvenkind
   that also gives Cloak of Displacement effect" creates
   incompatibility with other slot-conflicting items the player
   could otherwise wear).
3. Common slots (Head, Neck, Cloak, Boots, Gloves, Belt) are
   "competitive" — many strong items want these slots, so any new
   item competes for the same body real estate.
4. Slotless items (held in pockets, in bags, on belts as non-belt
   items) have no slot conflict but feel less identity-defining.
5. Ring slot is *highly* contested (max 2 rings) and is where many
   high-power items end up. Adding a ring is adding to a tight
   competition.

### Slot exclusivity (informal)

Some items effectively claim *more than their slot* by being so
identity-defining the player doesn't want to swap. A Vorpal sword
isn't physically in a slot, but the player won't carry a second
melee weapon. Design for the practical exclusivity, not just the
formal slot.

## 6. Reward Distribution Patterns

How items reach the players matters as much as which items they are.

### The Single Bestowal Pattern

One item, one defining moment of discovery. Best for Rare+ items.
The item arrives with a story; the player remembers it forever.

**When to use:** Rare, Very Rare, Legendary items. Items tied to
the campaign's central theme.

### The Hoard Pattern

A collection of items found together (dragon's hoard, lich's vault,
royal treasury). Players sort through and pick what suits them; the
items become part of the campaign's mythic moment.

**When to use:** End of a major arc; Tier 2 or higher; mixed rarities
(Common consumables + 1–2 Uncommon + 1 Rare typical for a hoard).

### The Drip Pattern

Items appear one at a time across many sessions, often as
incidental rewards. Less narratively heavy, keeps the magic flowing.

**When to use:** Tier 1; Common and Uncommon items; ongoing
exploration campaigns.

### The Quest Reward Pattern

The item is the goal of a quest, not its byproduct. Players know in
advance what they're seeking; the journey is about the *getting*.

**When to use:** Very Rare and Legendary items; identity-defining
class items; items tied to specific NPCs or factions.

### The Curse-Then-Reveal Pattern

The item is found and used before its full nature is known. The
party becomes invested; the reveal reframes the campaign.

**When to use:** Cursed-flavor items; sentient items; items with
hidden mythic significance.

## 7. Items Per Encounter — Treasure Density

How magical loot density relates to encounter design:

### Standard treasure density (DMG default)

- **Easy encounter:** No magic items expected
- **Medium encounter:** 0–1 Common/Uncommon item possible
- **Hard encounter:** 1–2 Common/Uncommon items, occasionally Rare
- **Deadly encounter:** 1–2 Uncommon/Rare items, occasionally Very Rare
- **Boss encounter (Tier 2+):** 1–2 Rare/Very Rare items + Common/Uncommon
- **Legendary encounter:** 1 Legendary item + supporting treasure

### Low-magic density adjustment

For "low magic" campaigns, divide expected items by 2 or 3. Each item
becomes a defining campaign moment.

### High-magic density adjustment

For "high magic" settings (e.g., Eberron, Spelljammer), items appear
at half the standard rate (more frequently). Each individual item
matters less; the *combination* of accumulated items defines the
character.

## 8. Quick Rarity-Selection Workflow

When designing or evaluating an item's rarity:

1. **What can the item do mechanically?**
   - One cosmetic effect → Common
   - One utility OR combat-relevant effect → Uncommon
   - 2 effects OR 1 strong combat effect → Rare
   - 2–3 notable effects, signature ability → Very Rare
   - 3+ effects with identity-defining signature → Legendary

2. **Does it require attunement?**
   - No → Common or Uncommon
   - Yes (simple prereq) → Uncommon or Rare
   - Yes (compound prereq) → Rare or Very Rare
   - Yes (compound + narrative ritual) → Legendary

3. **What tier should access it?**
   - Tier 1 character finds it → Common or Uncommon
   - Tier 2 character finds it → Uncommon or Rare
   - Tier 3 character finds it → Rare or Very Rare
   - Tier 4 character finds it → Very Rare or Legendary

4. **What's the campaign's magic density?**
   - High magic → push rarity one tier lower (the same item is more
     accessible)
   - Low magic → push rarity one tier higher (the same item is
     scarcer)

5. **Cross-check against existing official items:**
   - Open the DMG to a comparable item at each rarity tier
   - Where does your design fit naturally?

If the answer is ambiguous between two rarities, choose the **lower**.
Easier to bump up later via a variant than to nerf a player-favored
item.
