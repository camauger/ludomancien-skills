# Tone Examples

Examples of how to transform the same source material into different tones for read-aloud delivery.

## Source Material

```
The abandoned chapel sits at the edge of town. Broken stained glass. 
Pews overturned. Altar defaced. Used by cultists for midnight rituals. 
Symbols carved into walls (DC 15 Arcana to identify). 
Blood stains on floor (3 days old). Basement entrance hidden behind altar.
```

---

## Literary

**Characteristics:**
- Lyrical, layered prose with sensory depth
- Metaphor and symbolism woven naturally
- Rhythm and cadence matter (read aloud in your head)
- Present tense, second person for immediacy
- Questions probe emotional/philosophical angles
- 3-5 paragraphs for major scenes

**Example:**

> The chapel died years before anyone stopped calling it holy.
>
> You stand in what was once a sanctuary, and the word feels like ash on your tongue. Stained glass windows shattered long ago—jeweled light replaced by gray afternoon filtering through jagged teeth of colored glass still clinging to lead frames. The shards on the floor catch what little light remains, creating galaxies of ruby and sapphire among the dust.
>
> The pews lie scattered like broken ribs. Someone overturned them violently, or desperately, or with the kind of deliberate malice that makes destruction an art form. The altar—once white marble, you think—bears scars. Chisel marks. Scratches. Symbols you don't recognize carved deep enough to bleed meaning even if you can't read them. The stone itself seems wounded.
>
> The air tastes of old incense and older blood. Your eyes adjust to the dimness and pick out stains on the floorboards—dark patches that might be water damage, might be something else. Three days, if you had to guess. Maybe four. Long enough for the smell to fade, not long enough for the memory.
>
> Behind the altar, the shadows pool differently. A gap. A passage. Leading down.
>
> What kind of god were they worshiping here? And does it matter if it answered?

## Pulpy

**Characteristics:**
- Fast-paced, action-ready prose
- Vivid verbs and strong nouns
- Short, punchy sentences mixed with longer ones
- Present tense creates urgency
- Questions push toward action
- 2-3 paragraphs, keep moving

**Example:**

> The chapel's a corpse. You can smell it before you see it.
>
> Stained glass crunches under your boots—someone shattered every window. The pews? Kindling. Overturned, broken, scattered like a giant's temper tantrum. The altar's worse: gouged, defaced, carved with symbols that make your eyes water when you look at them too long. This wasn't vandalism. This was desecration with purpose.
>
> Blood on the floor. Old enough to turn black, fresh enough to still smell like copper and fear. Three days, maybe. The kind of stains that don't come from accidents. And everywhere—walls, floor, ceiling—those symbols. Crawling across every surface like infection. You don't need to be a scholar to know this place has been claimed by something that doesn't pray to any god you'd want to meet.
>
> There's a gap behind the altar. Darkness leading down. Basement? Crypt? Either way, that's where they went.
>
> Question is: are you going after them, or getting the hell out while you still can?

## Epic

**Characteristics:**
- Grand, mythic language
- Archetypal imagery and universal themes
- Elevated diction without being purple
- Can use second or third person
- Questions evoke destiny/fate/meaning
- 3-4 paragraphs, each building on the last

**Example:**

> Here stands a temple that has forgotten its god—or perhaps its god has forgotten it.
>
> You enter what was once a sanctuary, and the word rings hollow as bells in empty halls. The stained glass windows—once depicting saints and martyrs, now reduced to shards of colored memory—lie scattered across stone floors that have known centuries of prayer. But the prayers that echo here now are in no language the builders would recognize. The pews, hewn from ancient oak, lie broken like the oaths sworn upon them.
>
> Upon the altar—defiled, carved with signs that predate scripture—darkness has written its own gospel. Blood speaks its testament here, dark and patient on pale stone. Three days have passed since the last offering, though time moves strangely in places where the sacred and profane collide. The symbols cut into marble and wood are old, far older than this chapel, older perhaps than the faith that built these walls. They speak of powers that do not ask permission, of hungers that outlast civilizations.
>
> Beyond the desecrated altar, shadows gather with purpose. A passage descends into earth that has drunk too much sorrow. Here, in the space between what was holy and what has been claimed, a choice must be made.
>
> Will you descend into darkness to face what dwells below? Or will you leave this place to its new masters, knowing what they might become?

## Erotica

**Characteristics:**
- Sensual, intimate language
- Focus on physical sensations and desires
- Charged atmosphere, suggestive but not explicit
- Present tense, second person for intimacy
- Questions probe desire/temptation/taboo
- 2-4 paragraphs, building tension

**Example:**

> The chapel welcomes you with the promise of sin in a place meant for salvation.
>
> You step through the doorway and feel it immediately—that electric charge in the air when something forbidden becomes possible. Stained glass lies shattered on the floor, colored light now replaced by shadows that seem to breathe against your skin. The pews are overturned, scattered, and you can almost see it: bodies pressed against wood, hands grasping, prayers becoming moans. The air is thick with lingering incense and something else—sweat, perhaps. The musk of bodies and abandon.
>
> At the center: the altar. Once pristine white marble, now carved with symbols that make heat bloom in your chest. Someone took their time here, tracing each line with careful devotion, their fingers following curves and angles that suggest things the Church never taught. Blood stains the stone—dark, intimate, the kind that comes from willing sacrifice. You can imagine it: the blade, the gasp, the offering given in ecstasy.
>
> Behind the altar, darkness beckons. A passage leading down. Into what? A crypt? A ritual chamber? Some secret place where pleasure and devotion become indistinguishable?
>
> Your pulse quickens. Do you descend into that intimate darkness? Or do you turn away, leaving these mysteries untouched—at least for now?

## Mysterious

**Characteristics:**
- Suggestive, never fully explaining
- Emphasis on the unknown and unknowable
- Questions without answers
- Can be sparse or rich, but always elusive
- Present or past tense, maintain distance
- 2-3 paragraphs, withhold information

**Example:**

> The chapel keeps its secrets beneath a veneer of abandonment.
>
> You stand in the doorway, and something keeps you from entering immediately. Not fear. Not exactly. More like the sense that stepping inside would be acknowledging something you're not ready to acknowledge. The stained glass is gone—you notice this—but you're not certain when it shattered or why the shards remain scattered across the floor in patterns that almost suggest intention. The pews lie overturned. Perhaps there was violence. Perhaps there was ecstasy. Perhaps there was simply inevitability.
>
> The altar draws your eye despite—or because of—the marks carved into its surface. You don't recognize the symbols. That might be ignorance, or it might be wisdom. The dark stains on the floor could be three days old. Could be three years. Could be something that exists outside time as you understand it. Questions form and dissolve before you can voice them.
>
> Behind the altar: a gap in the shadows that might be a passage. Leading where? Down, certainly. Into what? That's a different question entirely. The darkness there isn't just absence of light—it's presence of something else.
>
> Do you enter? Or do you leave, understanding that some mysteries are meant to remain unsolved?

## Terse

**Characteristics:**
- Minimalist, economical prose
- Every word earns its place
- Strong images, no embellishment
- Present tense, second person
- Single sharp question at end
- 1-2 short paragraphs maximum

**Example:**

> Dead chapel. Windows smashed, pews destroyed, altar carved with symbols you don't recognize. Blood on the floor—three days old, maybe four. Stinks of old incense and something worse underneath.
>
> Someone's been using this place. Recently. The symbols on the walls aren't random: they're careful, methodical, purposeful. Behind the altar, there's a gap. Passage leading down into darkness.
>
> You going in?

---

## Selection Guide

**Literary**: Complex narratives, character-focused games, noir/gothic atmosphere, emotional depth

**Pulpy**: Action-heavy games, adventure tone, players who want urgency, modern/cinematic feel

**Epic**: High fantasy, mythic quests, grand campaigns, when scale matters, destiny/fate themes

**Erotica**: Mature themes, Velnaris-style content, romance focus, intimate character moments, tension-building

**Mysterious**: Horror games, investigation scenarios, Lovecraftian vibes, player-driven discovery, ambiguity as tool

**Terse**: Fast sessions, experienced players, OSR style, when pace matters more than atmosphere, combat-focused

---

## Mixing Tones

You can blend tones within a session:
- **Literary + Mysterious**: For sophisticated horror
- **Pulpy + Epic**: For mythic action-adventure
- **Terse + Mysterious**: For minimalist dread
- **Literary + Erotica**: For Velnaris-style atmospheric sensuality

Match tone to moment: epic for dramatic reveals, terse for action, literary for key scenes, mysterious for building dread.
