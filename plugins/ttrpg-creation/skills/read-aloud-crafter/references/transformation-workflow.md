# Transformation Workflow

Step-by-step process for converting game text into read-aloud narration.

## Core Process

### Step 1: Extract Core Information

Identify and preserve:
- **Location/situation essence**: What IS this place/moment?
- **Key physical details**: What must players know to interact?
- **Atmosphere markers**: Mood indicators in source text
- **Sensory anchors**: Existing smell/sound/texture mentions

Discard:
- All mechanical elements (DCs, skill checks, saving throws, damage)
- NPC stat blocks and mechanical abilities
- Secret/hidden information players shouldn't know yet
- Meta-commentary about game design
- References to stat blocks or mechanics sections

### Step 2: Determine Information Reveal Level

**Full Reveal (Standard)**
- Describe everything players would immediately perceive
- Include obvious features, people present, general atmosphere
- Suitable for: exploration, arrival at new location, setting scenes

**Partial Reveal (Investigation)**
- Describe surface details only
- Withhold specific clues or hidden elements
- Let players ask questions to reveal more
- Suitable for: mystery scenarios, detailed investigation scenes

**Minimal Reveal (Action)**
- Only essentials needed for immediate decision-making
- Focus on threats, exits, cover
- Suitable for: combat initiation, chase scenes, urgent situations

### Step 3: Expand Sensory Details

For chosen tone, add appropriate sensory layers:

**Visual**: Always include, but vary detail level
- Literary: Metaphoric imagery, light/shadow play, color symbolism
- Pulpy: Strong action verbs, clear spatial relationships
- Epic: Scale, grandeur, archetypal imagery
- Erotica: Body language, intimate details, suggestive lighting
- Mysterious: Partial glimpses, shadows, what's NOT seen
- Terse: Only most striking visual element

**Auditory**: Essential for immersion
- What sounds are present? (voices, music, nature, mechanical)
- What sounds are notably absent? (suspicious silence)
- Quality of sounds (echoing, muffled, sharp, melodic)

**Olfactory**: Most evocative sense, use strategically
- Good for: establishing atmosphere, memory triggers, disgust/desire
- Avoid: overuse (1-2 smell references per read-aloud maximum)
- Link to emotion or memory when possible

**Tactile**: Underused but powerful
- Air temperature and moisture
- Textures players would feel (rough stone, smooth silk)
- Physical sensations (goosebumps, tension, warmth)

**Taste**: Use sparingly
- "The air tastes of..." (not literal taste)
- For specific situations (near ocean, in smoke, fear responses)

### Step 4: Structure for Oral Delivery

**Paragraph 1: Arrival/Immediate Impression**
- Establish WHERE and WHEN
- Create emotional hook
- 1-3 sentences establishing tone

**Paragraph 2-3: Expanding Detail**
- Layer in sensory details
- Reveal important elements naturally
- Build atmosphere progressively
- Each paragraph focuses on one aspect (space, inhabitants, hidden details)

**Paragraph 4 (if needed): The Hook**
- Something that demands attention
- A question raised
- An element requiring decision

**Final Sentence: Engagement Question**
- Opens player agency
- Invites action or reaction
- Matches tone (philosophical for literary, urgent for pulpy)

### Step 5: Point of View Alignment

**Second Person Present**: Standard for read-alouds
- "You enter the chapel..."
- Creates immediacy and immersion
- Players become "I" automatically

**Passive Voice Sparingly**:
- "The door stands open" vs "Someone opened the door"
- Use when actor is mysterious or irrelevant
- Creates sense of events happening around players

**Active Voice Preferred**:
- "Shadows dance across walls" not "Walls are covered in dancing shadows"
- More immediate, more engaging

### Step 6: Rhythm and Pacing

**Sentence Length Variation**:
- Short sentences: urgency, impact, clarity
- Medium sentences: standard description
- Long sentences: building tension, complex imagery
- Alternate lengths for musicality

**Paragraph Breaks**:
- Visual rest for eyes
- Emotional beats
- Scene shifts
- Give GM breathing space when reading

**Comma Placement**:
- Creates natural pauses for oral delivery
- Use more commas than in written prose
- Read aloud to test pacing

### Step 7: Tone Application

Apply chosen tone's characteristics (see tone-examples.md for detailed guidance):

**Literary**:
- Metaphor and symbolism
- Layered meanings
- Philosophical undertones
- Beauty in darkness

**Pulpy**:
- Action verbs
- Concrete details
- Forward momentum
- Clear stakes

**Epic**:
- Elevated language
- Mythic resonance
- Grand scale
- Universal themes

**Erotica**:
- Charged atmosphere
- Physical awareness
- Desire/temptation
- Intimate details

**Mysterious**:
- Withhold information
- Suggestive rather than explicit
- Embrace ambiguity
- Questions without answers

**Terse**:
- Minimal words
- Maximum impact
- No flourishes
- Pure function

### Step 8: Craft Engagement Question

End with question that:
- Invites immediate response
- Matches tone (don't undercut mood)
- Opens possibilities (avoid yes/no when possible)
- Returns agency to players

**Good Examples**:
- "What draws your attention first?"
- "Where do you go?"
- "How do you approach?"
- "What do you do?"

**Tone-Specific**:
- Literary: "What does this place remind you of?"
- Pulpy: "What's your move?"
- Epic: "Will you face what awaits below?"
- Erotica: "What do you desire here?"
- Mysterious: "What are you afraid to discover?"
- Terse: "Well?"

## Common Pitfalls

**Over-Description**:
- Players zone out after 3 paragraphs
- Save detail for when they ask
- Hit highlights, trust their imagination

**Purple Prose**:
- Not every word needs to be fancy
- Literary ≠ pretentious
- "The crimson-hued sanguine effusion" → "Blood"

**Telling Emotions**:
- Don't say "You feel scared"
- Describe what creates fear
- Let players generate emotion

**Breaking Immersion**:
- Avoid "You notice" or "You see" excessively
- Just describe what's there
- Exception: calling attention to specific detail

**Inconsistent Tone**:
- If you start literary, stay literary
- Don't switch mid-read-aloud
- Tone shifts happen between scenes, not during

**Buried Lede**:
- Most important element shouldn't be last
- Unless deliberately building to reveal
- Players need to know what matters

## Quick Reference

**Literary**: Poetic, layered, metaphoric, 3-5 paragraphs
**Pulpy**: Active, vivid, urgent, 2-3 paragraphs  
**Epic**: Grand, mythic, elevated, 3-4 paragraphs
**Erotica**: Sensual, charged, intimate, 2-4 paragraphs
**Mysterious**: Suggestive, ambiguous, elusive, 2-3 paragraphs
**Terse**: Minimal, sharp, functional, 1-2 paragraphs

Always: Sensory details + engagement question + oral delivery pacing
Never: Mechanics, secrets, stat blocks, or "you feel [emotion]"
