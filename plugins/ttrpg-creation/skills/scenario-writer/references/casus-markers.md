# Casus Belli Markers

GM guidance boxes that distinguish professional scenarios from amateur adventures. These formatting conventions improve usability at the table.

## En Bref (Executive Summary)

Appears at the very beginning of the scenario, after the Adventure Card. Tells the GM everything in a structured format.

### Format

```markdown
> **📋 En Bref**
>
> [Who] is doing [what] in [where] because [why]. The PCs get involved 
> when [inciting incident]. They'll need to [main objective] while 
> dealing with [main obstacle/antagonist].
>
> **Le twist**: [The complication that makes this interesting]
>
> **Victoire**: [What success looks like]
> **Échec**: [What failure looks like]
```

### Example (Noir Investigation)

```markdown
> **📋 En Bref**
>
> Bishop Morvaine confessed to making a deal with the entity beneath 
> Velnaris, offering to help it awaken in exchange for power. Cirelle 
> Noar, a Confessor's Companion at the House of Red Vows, heard this 
> confession three nights ago and fled in terror. She's now in hiding 
> somewhere in the city, carrying a truth that could destroy the Church 
> and accelerate the entity's awakening.
>
> The PCs get involved when Madame Lirael approaches them in the Pleasure 
> Quarter, seeking help to find Cirelle before she's silenced permanently. 
> They'll need to navigate the Quarter's information economy, locate 
> Cirelle, and decide whether to expose the truth or protect the secret.
>
> **Le twist**: The confession implicates others beyond Morvaine—names 
> Cirelle didn't write down but remembers. Multiple factions want her 
> silenced for different reasons. The truth, if revealed, will shatter 
> the city's fragile balance.
>
> **Victoire**: Cirelle is found and protected. The party makes an informed 
> choice about the truth, whether exposing it, protecting it, or negotiating 
> a compromise.
> **Échec**: Cirelle is silenced permanently. The conspiracy continues 
> unchecked. The party becomes marked as loose ends.
```

### Example (Dungeon Crawl)

```markdown
> **📋 En Bref**
>
> A desperate alchemist named Fen Holloway is kidnapping street children 
> to fuel a resurrection ritual in the abandoned Miremark Asylum. He's 
> trying to bring back his daughter, dead from plague six months ago. 
> The PCs get involved when they witness an abduction or are hired by 
> worried parents.
>
> **Le twist**: Fen has already succeeded once—his "daughter" is now 
> a soulless husk that hungers for life energy. He keeps trying, not 
> understanding why she came back wrong.
>
> **Victoire**: Stop Fen, rescue the children, put the daughter to rest
> **Échec**: More children taken, Fen completes a larger ritual, 
> something worse comes through
```

## Ambiance (Atmosphere)

Helps the GM create mood. Appears before or within scenes that need specific tone.

### Format

```markdown
> **🎭 Ambiance**
>
> [Specific GM instructions for tone, pacing, or delivery]
> [Optional: music/sound suggestions]
> [Optional: physical prop or visual suggestions]
```

### Examples

```markdown
> **🎭 Ambiance**
>
> This scene should feel claustrophobic. Speak quietly, lean in, 
> describe the walls as "too close." If the players start joking, 
> have dust fall from the ceiling—nothing dangerous, just a reminder 
> that they're underground in an unstable space.
```

```markdown
> **🎭 Ambiance**
>
> Play the villain as genuinely charming. They believe they're the 
> hero of their own story. Don't sneer or monologue—be warm, 
> reasonable, almost likeable. The horror comes from recognizing 
> how close the PCs might be to making the same choices.
```

```markdown
> **🎭 Ambiance**
>
> **Music**: Low strings, heartbeat rhythm (horror film tension)
> **Lighting**: If playing in person, dim the lights for this scene
> **Pacing**: Slow down. Let silences hang. Answer questions with 
> questions. Make them work for every piece of information.
```

## Tactics (Combat Guidance)

Appears in combat scenes. Tells GM how enemies fight.

### Format

```markdown
> **⚔️ Tactics**
>
> **Opening**: [How combat begins, surprise elements]
> **Focus**: [Who enemies target and why]
> **Adaptation**: [How enemies respond to PC tactics]
> **Breaking point**: [When/why enemies flee or surrender]
```

### Examples

```markdown
> **⚔️ Tactics**
>
> **Opening**: The cultists spring from hidden positions (Stealth +4 vs 
> passive Perception). They've prepared: oil on the floor (DC 12 Dex or 
> fall prone), ready actions to attack whoever enters first.
>
> **Focus**: Kill the spellcaster. They've dealt with adventurers before 
> and know where the danger comes from. Two cultists will attempt to 
> grapple and drag the caster away from the group.
>
> **Adaptation**: If clearly losing, the leader triggers the trap early 
> (ceiling collapse, everyone makes DC 14 Dex save or 3d6 bludgeoning).
>
> **Breaking point**: Cultists fight to the death (true believers). The 
> leader will attempt to flee to warn the high priest if reduced below 
> 10 HP.
```

```markdown
> **⚔️ Tactics**
>
> These are mercenaries, not zealots. They'll:
> - Test the party's strength in round 1
> - Focus fire on wounded targets
> - Demand surrender if they're winning by round 3
> - Flee at 50% casualties (they're not paid enough to die)
> - Surrender if cornered, offer information for their lives
>
> Their employer's identity is valuable leverage—they know it.
```

## Adaptation (System Conversion)

For multi-system scenarios. Provides conversion notes for other popular games.

### Format

```markdown
> **🔄 Adaptation**
>
> **[System Name]**: [Specific conversion notes]
> **[System Name]**: [Specific conversion notes]
```

### Examples

```markdown
> **🔄 Adaptation**
>
> **Pathfinder 2e**: Use the same DCs. Cult Assassins become level 4 
> creatures. Replace the Water Weird with an Elemental (water).
>
> **Call of Cthulhu**: This works as a modern-day scenario with minimal 
> changes. Replace magic with ritual components. The resurrection becomes 
> a Mi-Go brain cylinder procedure gone wrong. Sanity loss for witnessing 
> the "daughter": 1/1d6.
>
> **Chroniques Oubliées**: Les statistiques fonctionnent directement. 
> Remplacez les références au Manuel des Monstres par le Bestiaire CO.
```

```markdown
> **🔄 Adaptation**
>
> **System-Neutral**: If using a narrative system without detailed stats:
> - Guard: Competent but not exceptional. Fails against trickery.
> - Assassin: Dangerous one-on-one. Flees from fair fights.
> - The Creature: Terrifying. Direct combat should feel like a last resort.
> - Social challenges: Require leverage, evidence, or significant risk.
```

## Warning (Content Advisory)

For scenes with potentially sensitive content. Allows GMs to prepare or adjust.

### Format

```markdown
> **⚠️ Avertissement**
>
> This scene contains [content type]. Consider:
> - [Adjustment option for comfort]
> - [Alternative approach if needed]
```

### Example

```markdown
> **⚠️ Avertissement**
>
> This scene involves child endangerment (kidnapped children in cages). 
> The children are alive and can be rescued, but the imagery may be 
> disturbing.
>
> **Pour atténuer**: Describe the cages as "cells" and the children as 
> "frightened but unharmed." Skip detailed descriptions of their condition.
>
> **Alternative**: The victims are young adults (apprentices) instead of 
> children. Same emotional stakes, less potentially triggering imagery.
```

## Variante (Alternative Path)

For branch points where different player choices lead to different experiences.

### Format

```markdown
> **🔀 Variante**
>
> **Si les PJ [choice A]**: [Consequence and direction]
> **Si les PJ [choice B]**: [Consequence and direction]
```

### Example

```markdown
> **🔀 Variante**
>
> **Si les PJ arrivent rapidement**: They reach Cirelle before The Shadow. 
> They have time to talk, to make a choice, to plan. The Shadow arrives 
> later, creating a tense confrontation.
>
> **Si les PJ prennent trop de temps**: The Shadow arrives simultaneously 
> or just before PCs. Combat or negotiation becomes necessary. Cirelle is 
> in immediate danger.
>
> **Si les PJ alertent les mauvaises personnes**: Bishop's agents arrive 
> instead of or alongside The Shadow. Multiple factions converge.
```

## Branching Endings (Resolution Framework)

For scenarios with meaningful player choices, provide multiple distinct endings rather than simple success/failure.

### Format

```markdown
## Branching Endings

**Ending A: [Descriptive Name]** (If players [choice])
[What happens]. [Immediate consequences].
*Consequence*: [Long-term impact]. [Thematic question raised].

**Ending B: [Descriptive Name]** (If players [choice])
[What happens]. [Immediate consequences].
*Consequence*: [Long-term impact]. [Thematic question raised].

**Ending C: [Descriptive Name]** (If players [choice])
[What happens]. [Immediate consequences].
*Consequence*: [Long-term impact]. [Thematic question raised].

**Ending D: Failure State** (If [failure condition])
[What happens]. [Immediate consequences].
*Consequence*: [Long-term impact]. [What options remain].
```

### Example (Noir Investigation)

```markdown
## Branching Endings

**Ending A: The Truth Exposed** (If players choose to reveal the confession)
They convince Cirelle to testify. They expose Bishop Morvaine's conspiracy. 
The city fractures. The Church splinters. Cirelle survives, but she becomes 
a target. The party gains powerful enemies—and powerful allies.
*Consequence*: The city's balance shifts. Corruption spreads in new ways. 
But truth is served. The question becomes: was it worth it?

**Ending B: The Secret Protected** (If players choose to protect the secret)
They help Cirelle escape Velnaris or go into permanent hiding. They maintain 
the secret. The city's fragile stability is preserved. Bishop Morvaine remains 
in power. The conspiracy continues.
*Consequence*: The city remains stable. But the entity's influence grows 
unchecked. The party carries the weight of complicity. The question becomes: 
can they live with what they've done?

**Ending C: The Compromise** (If players negotiate a middle path)
They arrange a deal—Cirelle keeps the secret in exchange for protection. 
Bishop Morvaine is blackmailed into changing his behavior without public 
exposure. The truth remains hidden. But the conspiracy is disrupted.
*Consequence*: Balance is maintained. But everyone involved is compromised. 
The question becomes: are they any better than the people they're fighting?

**Ending D: Failure State** (If Cirelle is captured or killed)
They arrive too late. Cirelle is silenced permanently. The secret remains 
hidden. But the party knows the truth. Powerful forces now see them as 
loose ends.
*Consequence*: The conspiracy continues. The party is marked. They must 
decide whether to continue fighting or flee. The question becomes: what 
do they do now?
```

**Key Principle**: Each ending should feel like a legitimate conclusion with its own weight and consequences, not just "good ending" vs "bad ending."

## Placement Guidelines

| Marker | Placement |
|--------|-----------|
| En Bref | Document start, after Adventure Card |
| Ambiance | Before or within scenes needing specific tone |
| Tactics | Within combat scenes, after read-aloud |
| Adaptation | Document end, or after relevant scenes |
| Warning | Before scenes with sensitive content |
| Variante | At significant decision points |
| Running Notes | Near document end, before Technical Appendix |

## Running Notes (GM Atmosphere Guide)

A dedicated section providing thematic guidance, opening text, and pacing recommendations. Appears near the end of the scenario, after the flowchart.

### Format

```markdown
## Running Notes

### Opening Atmosphere
*[Evocative opening text the GM can read or paraphrase]*

### Key Themes to Emphasize
- **[Theme 1]**: [How to convey it at the table]
- **[Theme 2]**: [How to convey it at the table]
- **[Theme 3]**: [How to convey it at the table]

### Pacing Guidelines
- **Act 1 (25%)**: [Time estimate]. [Focus]
- **Act 2 (50%)**: [Time estimate]. [Focus]
- **Act 3 (25%)**: [Time estimate]. [Focus]

### Safety Reminders (if applicable)
- [Relevant safety tools and when to use them]
```

### Example

```markdown
## Running Notes

### Opening Atmosphere

*Dusk settles over Velnaris like a lover's sigh—slow, deliberate, inevitable.*

*The Pleasure Quarter awakens as the sun dies. Crimson lanterns bloom along 
the Serpent's Coil like flowers that only open in darkness. The air carries 
jasmine and opium smoke, spilled wine and expensive perfume, the musk of 
bodies and the sharper scent of desperation.*

*You stand at the threshold of something. The question is: do you step 
forward, or turn back?*

### Key Themes to Emphasize

- **Secrets as Currency**: Information has value. Every question costs 
  something. Every answer reveals more questions. In Velnaris, knowledge 
  is power, but power always comes with a price.

- **Moral Complexity**: There are no clean choices. Every path has 
  consequences. The "right" thing might destroy everything. The "wrong" 
  thing might save it.

- **Intimate Stakes**: This isn't about saving the world—it's about 
  protecting one person, making one choice, carrying one secret.

- **Atmosphere as Character**: The city itself is a character. Use sensory 
  details—the way candlelight makes shadows confess what daylight hides, 
  the way jasmine and opium smoke mix with spilled wine.

### Pacing Guidelines

- **Act 1 (25%)**: 45-60 minutes. Establish atmosphere, introduce mystery, 
  provide first clues.
- **Act 2 (50%)**: 90-120 minutes. Investigation, social encounters, 
  information gathering, revelation.
- **Act 3 (25%)**: 45-60 minutes. Confrontation, choice, resolution, 
  consequences.

### Safety Reminders

- Use fade-to-black for intimate scenes
- Check in with players about comfort levels
- Use X-Card if anyone needs to skip content
- Emphasize that NPCs respect boundaries
```

## Visual Formatting

All markers use:
- Blockquote formatting (`>`)
- Emoji icon for quick visual identification
- Bold header
- Consistent internal structure

This allows easy scanning during play and distinguishes GM-only content from player-facing text.
