# Power and Influence Mechanics

Systems for quantifying and tracking faction capabilities, resources, and influence.

## Overview

Factions need mechanical representation to:
- Create meaningful player choices
- Track faction growth/decline
- Determine action success
- Model power struggles
- Generate plot complications

Choose complexity level based on campaign needs.

---

## System 1: Narrative (Minimal Mechanics)

**Best for:** Story-focused campaigns, when mechanics would slow play, GM comfort over rules

**Method:** Track faction power qualitatively through descriptive tiers and narrative consequences.

### Power Tiers

**Tier 5 - Dominant**
- Controls territory/domain absolutely
- Can act with near-impunity
- Resources seem unlimited
- Other factions defer or hide

**Tier 4 - Major**
- Significant territory/influence
- Can openly challenge authorities
- Substantial resources
- Commands respect and fear

**Tier 3 - Established**
- Recognized presence
- Must be cautious of authorities
- Adequate resources
- Cannot be ignored

**Tier 2 - Emerging**
- Building power base
- Must operate carefully
- Limited resources
- Can be suppressed if noticed

**Tier 1 - Nascent**
- Just forming
- Must hide activities
- Scarce resources
- Easily destroyed

**Usage:**
- Track faction tier and direction (ascending/stable/declining)
- Major PC actions or events shift tiers
- Use tiers to determine what factions can accomplish

**Example:**
> The Thieves' Guild is Tier 4 (Major) and Ascending. They can afford to bribe city officials, have safe houses throughout the district, and the Watch avoids direct confrontation. PC actions exposing their leader might drop them to Tier 3.

## System 2: Resource Tracks (Light Mechanics)

**Best for:** Campaigns wanting some crunch without complex math, tracking faction capabilities

**Method:** Track 3-5 resource types, rated 1-10. Resources enable actions and can be depleted.

### Resource Types

**Wealth** - Financial assets
- Enables: Bribes, equipment, hiring, expansion
- Depleted by: Large purchases, economic warfare, seized assets
- Recovered by: Income streams, heists, trade

**Influence** - Social/political power
- Enables: Favors, protection, information, law manipulation
- Depleted by: Scandal, failure, overuse, rival action
- Recovered by: Favors repaid, blackmail, success, time

**Force** - Military/coercive capacity
- Enables: Violence, protection, intimidation, territory control
- Depleted by: Casualties, desertion, defeat, suppression
- Recovered by: Recruitment, training, victory, morale

**Information** - Knowledge and intelligence
- Enables: Blackmail, advantage, preparation, counter-intel
- Depleted by: Exposure, captured agents, misdirection
- Recovered by: Spying, infiltration, analysis, informants

**Magic** (if applicable) - Supernatural capability
- Enables: Rituals, wards, scrying, enchantment
- Depleted by: Casting, artifacts destroyed, mages lost
- Recovered by: Recruitment, discovery, recharge time

### Rating Scale

**10** - Dominant in this resource (nearly unlimited)
**7-9** - Strong (can use freely with rare strain)
**4-6** - Adequate (can use but must be strategic)
**2-3** - Weak (severe limitations, must husband resources)
**1** - Critical (nearly depleted, desperate)
**0** - Exhausted (cannot use this resource until recovered)

### Action Costs

**Minor Action** - Cost 0-1 (routine operation)
- Bribe a guard
- Gather basic information
- Small-scale violence
- Single-use magic

**Significant Action** - Cost 2-3 (stretches resources)
- Bribe an official
- Extensive intelligence operation
- Squad-level combat
- Ritual magic

**Major Action** - Cost 4-6 (substantial commitment)
- Bribe a magistrate
- Long-term infiltration
- Company-level combat
- Powerful permanent magic

**Critical Action** - Cost 7+ (all-in gambit)
- Bribe a council member
- Topple rival organization
- Army-level combat
- Reality-altering magic

### Resource Recovery

- **Passive**: +1 per downtime period (week/month)
- **Active**: PC actions or events restore 1-3
- **Crisis**: Resources can drop by 2-5 suddenly

**Example:**

> **The Crimson Blades** (Mercenary Company)
> - Wealth: 6 (adequate, recent payday)
> - Influence: 4 (some reputation, not connected)
> - Force: 8 (strong, well-trained and equipped)
> - Information: 3 (weak, no spy network)
> 
> They can easily deploy soldiers (Force 8, low cost) but bribing officials (Wealth 6, cost 2-3) will strain resources. They can't engage in intelligence warfare (Information 3) without help.

## System 3: Domain Control (Territorial)

**Best for:** Campaigns focused on territory, city-districts, or regional control

**Method:** Divide territory into zones. Track faction control per zone (0-5 points).

### Control Levels Per Zone

**5 - Absolute Control**
- Faction operates openly without opposition
- Controls all businesses, services, protection
- Residents defer to faction as government
- Other factions cannot operate without permission

**4 - Dominant**
- Faction is primary power but not unopposed
- Controls most major operations
- Residents recognize as power
- Other factions operate in shadows or with permission

**3 - Contested**
- Multiple factions vie for control
- No clear dominant power
- Residents navigate between factions
- Violence and intrigue common

**2 - Presence**
- Faction operates but doesn't control
- Must negotiate with dominant faction
- Residents aware but not controlled by faction
- Can be pushed out by dominant power

**1 - Foothold**
- Minimal presence, establishing operations
- Must hide from dominant faction
- Residents barely aware of faction
- Easily eliminated if discovered

**0 - No Presence**
- Faction has no operations in zone
- Controlled by others or neutral

### Zone Actions

**Expand** (Cost varies by opposition)
- Increase control in adjacent zone
- Requires resources appropriate to method
- Opposed by factions with presence

**Consolidate** (Moderate cost)
- Increase control in current zone
- Build infrastructure, recruit, establish presence
- Less direct opposition than expansion

**Sabotage** (Variable cost)
- Reduce rival's control in zone
- Indirect, can be covert
- Risk of retaliation

**Defend** (Triggered response)
- Prevent control loss
- React to rival actions
- Can be passive (infrastructure) or active (force)

### Victory Conditions

Campaign can end when:
- One faction controls X zones absolutely
- Faction achieves control threshold across territory
- All rival factions eliminated from region
- Negotiated peace establishes zones

**Example Map:**

```
Velnaris Districts (Control):

Pleasure Quarter:
- House of Red Vows: 4 (Dominant)
- Thieves' Guild: 2 (Presence)
- Church Inquisitors: 1 (Foothold)

Cathedral District:
- Church: 5 (Absolute)
- Thieves' Guild: 0 (None)
- Revolutionary Cells: 1 (Foothold - hidden)

Gilded Exchange:
- Merchant Consortium: 4 (Dominant)
- Noble Houses: 3 (Contested with Merchants)
- Thieves' Guild: 2 (Presence)
```

## System 4: Influence Web (Relationship-Based)

**Best for:** Intrigue-heavy campaigns, political maneuvering, social dynamics

**Method:** Track relationships between factions as network of obligations, alliances, and enmities.

### Relationship Types

**Alliance (Positive 3)**
- Mutual support, shared goals
- Provide resources to each other
- Defend against common enemies
- May still have internal tensions

**Partnership (Positive 2)**
- Cooperative on specific issues
- Limited resource sharing
- Non-aggression pact
- Pragmatic, not ideological

**Cordial (Positive 1)**
- Friendly but no commitment
- Will help if costs are low
- Prefer negotiation to conflict
- Neutral default state

**Neutral (0)**
- No strong relationship
- Will deal if mutually beneficial
- Indifferent to each other's fate
- Most common state

**Tense (Negative 1)**
- Distrust, potential conflict
- Avoid cooperation
- May obstruct minor actions
- One incident from hostility

**Hostile (Negative 2)**
- Active opposition
- Attack when advantageous
- Seek to weaken without open war
- May still negotiate under duress

**War (Negative 3)**
- All-out conflict
- Commit significant resources
- Seek destruction of rival
- No negotiation without defeat

### Relationship Shifts

Actions change relationships:
- **Alliance**: Major mutual victory, marriage, treaty (+2 or +3)
- **Aid**: Help against rival, share intelligence (+1)
- **Betrayal**: Break agreement, attack without warning (-2 or -3)
- **Offense**: Insult, theft, violence against members (-1)
- **Time**: Grudges fade slowly (+1 per significant time period if no contact)

### Influence Through Relationships

Factions can act through positive relationships:
- Request favors based on relationship strength
- Combine resources for major actions
- Share intelligence and capabilities
- Political cover for unpopular actions

Factions suffer from negative relationships:
- Hostile factions actively oppose actions
- War drains resources on both sides
- Must defend multiple fronts if many enemies
- Allies pressure to resolve conflicts

**Example Web:**

```
Church <--Alliance--> Noble Houses
   |                      |
Hostile              Hostile
   |                      |
Revolutionaries <--Partnership--> Thieves' Guild
   |                      |
Tense                Cordial
   |                      |
Merchants <--Neutral--> House of Red Vows
```

## System 5: Action Economy (Complex)

**Best for:** Mechanically-inclined groups, campaign where faction play is central, clear rules

**Method:** Factions have Action Points per turn (month/season). Actions cost AP. Track resources and influence numerically.

### Action Points Per Turn

**Nascent Faction**: 2 AP
**Small Faction**: 3 AP
**Medium Faction**: 4 AP
**Large Faction**: 5 AP
**Dominant Faction**: 6 AP

Modifiers:
- +1 AP if well-organized
- +1 AP if wealthy
- -1 AP if in crisis
- -1 AP if leadership weak

### Action Types and Costs

**Free Actions** (0 AP)
- Routine operations within controlled territory
- Passive defense
- Maintain status quo

**Minor Actions** (1 AP each)
- Gather information
- Move resources
- Minor violence or coercion
- Establish contact with faction
- Propaganda campaign

**Standard Actions** (2 AP each)
- Major operation (heist, assassination, raid)
- Expand influence into new area
- Negotiate treaty
- Recruit significant members
- Research/develop capability

**Major Actions** (3 AP each)
- Large-scale warfare
- Coup or revolution
- Massive construction project
- Fundamental strategic shift
- Alliance formation

**Reactions** (Special)
- Can spend 1 AP to respond to rival action
- Interrupt enemy action
- Must have AP remaining
- Declare before action resolves

### Turn Sequence

1. **Upkeep**: Collect income, handle consequences
2. **Planning**: Secretly assign AP to actions
3. **Resolution**: Reveal and resolve actions in initiative order
4. **Consequences**: Determine results, shifts in power

### PC Integration

- PCs can assist faction actions (bonus to roll)
- PCs can undertake actions for faction (no AP cost)
- PC success/failure affects faction reputation
- Factions can spend AP to help PCs

**Example Turn:**

> **The Bloody Daggers** (Criminal Gang - Medium, 4 AP)
>
> Turn Actions:
> 1. Establish smuggling route (2 AP) - Expand wealth income
> 2. Assassinate rival lieutenant (2 AP) - Weaken enemy
> 3. Keep 0 AP for reaction
>
> If rivals attack, cannot react (no AP remaining).
> Both actions require success checks or contested rolls.

## Mixing Systems

Most campaigns benefit from combining systems:

**Narrative + Resource Tracks**
- Use tiers for big picture
- Use resources for specific actions
- Best of both worlds

**Domain Control + Relationship Web**
- Territory for physical control
- Relationships for political complexity
- Full political simulation

**Resource Tracks + Action Economy**
- Resources enable actions
- Action economy prevents infinite moves
- Balanced crunch

---

## Campaign Integration

### Short Campaign (1-10 sessions)
Use Narrative system only. Keep it simple.

### Medium Campaign (10-30 sessions)
Use Resource Tracks or Domain Control. Enough depth without overwhelming.

### Long Campaign (30+ sessions)
Consider Action Economy or mixing systems. Complexity pays off over time.

### Intrigue-Focused
Influence Web is essential regardless of other systems.

### War-Focused
Domain Control + Resource Tracks work well.

### Noir/Mystery
Keep mechanics light, use Narrative primarily.

---

## GM Tips

**Don't Track Everything:** Only track factions PCs interact with regularly.

**Abstract NPC Factions:** NPC vs. NPC faction conflict can be narrated results, not rolled.

**PC Actions Matter:** Faction mechanics exist to make PC choices meaningful, not to create spreadsheet simulator.

**Adjust On Fly:** If system isn't working, simplify or change mid-campaign.

**Telegraph Power:** Players should understand relative faction strength through description, not just numbers.

**Consequences Have Weight:** Faction defeat, triumph, or shift should affect the world visibly.
