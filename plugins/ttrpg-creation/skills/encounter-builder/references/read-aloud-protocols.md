# Read-Aloud Protocols for Encounters

Templates and patterns for the read-aloud text that frames an
encounter's entrance and resolution.

This skill produces *functional* read-aloud — text that orients
players, sets tone, and starts the scene. For *polished, publication-
ready* read-aloud, run `read-aloud-crafter` after this skill.

---

## 1. What Read-Aloud Should Do

A good encounter read-aloud accomplishes four jobs in 80–150 words:

1. **Orient** — where are the PCs, what time of day, what's
   immediately visible
2. **Establish stakes** — what's at risk, what's at play, who else
   is present
3. **Set tone** — atmosphere, mood, sensory texture
4. **Invite action** — end with something that gives the players
   their first move

A read-aloud that does all four is *short* (a paragraph or two);
a read-aloud that tries to do more drifts into exposition.

### What read-aloud should NOT do

- **Expound on backstory** — that's the GM's job after the players
  ask questions
- **Force a feeling** — "you feel afraid" doesn't work; *describe
  what produces fear* and let the players feel it
- **Make decisions for PCs** — "you draw your sword" is the
  player's choice, not the read-aloud's
- **Reveal hidden information** — what the PCs can't yet perceive
  doesn't belong in the entrance read-aloud
- **Over-describe trivia** — every minor object becomes noise

## 2. Entrance Read-Aloud — Patterns by Encounter Type

### Combat encounter entrance

The PCs face a hostile situation. The read-aloud orients them to
the threat, the space, and the immediate decision.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: spatial orientation — where the PCs are entering]
[1–2 sentences: who/what is here, and what they look like / are doing]
[1 sentence: the threat or the moment of transition into combat]
[1 sentence: a sensory detail — sound, smell, visual that anchors the
scene]
[Optional 1 sentence: a feature of the terrain or environment that
hints at tactical options]
```

**Example (mid-level combat in an old library):**

> The double doors swing open into a vaulted reading room, dust
> motes drifting in the slanted light from broken windows three
> floors up. Three figures in dark robes stand around a circle of
> books arranged on the floor, their hands raised mid-incantation.
> The nearest one turns, eyes white with a thin sheen of frost. A
> wave of cold rolls outward from the circle, fogging your breath.
> A chandelier hangs crooked from the ceiling — barely.

This entrance:
- Orients (vaulted reading room, broken windows)
- Establishes threat (three cultists mid-ritual, hostile turn)
- Sets tone (cold, oppressive, ancient)
- Invites action (the chandelier is mentioned — players will notice
  the tactical option)

### Social encounter entrance

The PCs meet someone. The read-aloud orients them to the setting,
the NPC, and the social context.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: spatial orientation]
[1–2 sentences: the NPC's appearance and immediate manner]
[1 sentence: what the NPC is doing or says first]
[Optional: a detail about other people present, or about the room's
political weight]
```

**Example (negotiating with a merchant in a back room):**

> The shop's main floor is dim; the back room behind the bead-curtain
> is darker still, lit by a single oil lamp. Mavren sits at a small
> table with a half-empty cup of strong coffee in front of him, his
> hands folded tightly. He doesn't rise when you enter. "You're
> later than I expected," he says, without looking up. A second cup
> waits across from him. It has not been poured.

This entrance:
- Orients (dim back room, lamp, table)
- Establishes the NPC (Mavren, tense, deliberately undeferential)
- Sets tone (cautious, suspicious, formal)
- Invites action (the unpoured cup is a small but loaded detail)

### Exploration encounter entrance

The PCs face an environmental or navigational challenge. The
read-aloud orients them to the obstacle and the stakes.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: spatial orientation — where the PCs are]
[1–2 sentences: the environment in vivid sensory terms]
[1 sentence: the obstacle / challenge — what blocks the goal]
[Optional: a glimpse of the goal beyond, or a hint at multiple paths]
```

**Example (crossing a chasm in a dwarven ruin):**

> The corridor opens onto a chasm that splits the floor of the great
> hall. Faint emerald light rises from below — the breath of the
> deep, dwarves once called it, a glow from something living far
> down. The far side is forty feet across. A single iron bridge
> stretches over the gap, but its center has collapsed inward,
> leaving a gap of perhaps ten feet between sections. The bridge
> creaks even now, settling against the weight that isn't there.

This entrance:
- Orients (corridor opens onto a chasm)
- Establishes the environment (emerald light, scale, ancient
  dwarven ruin)
- Sets the challenge (collapsed bridge, ten-foot gap)
- Sets tone (mysterious, ancient, dangerous)

### Chase encounter entrance

The PCs see the quarry / pursuer and the chase begins. The read-
aloud orients them to the initial situation and the route's first
segment.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: the moment of recognition — the quarry is seen, the
pursuer is heard]
[1 sentence: spatial setup — where, distance, route ahead]
[1 sentence: a complication or feature of the immediate environment]
[Optional: a verbal cue from quarry / pursuer if appropriate]
```

**Example (urban chase, quarry just spotted):**

> She bolts, knocking over a basket of dried fish as she goes. The
> alley is narrow, slick with rain, and twists out of sight to the
> left after twenty feet. A market crowd thickens further ahead. You
> hear her boots on the cobblestones, fast and rhythmic — and behind
> you, the city watch's whistle starting up, three sharp blasts.

This entrance:
- Orients (alley, narrow, slick, market beyond)
- Establishes the chase (quarry running, lead established)
- Sets tone (rain, slippery, urgent)
- Adds complication (watch responding)

### Downtime encounter entrance

Downtime encounters often span days. The read-aloud frames the
*decision point* — the PC starts the activity with a specific
moment.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: setting the scene of the activity's start]
[1 sentence: the PC's mood or focus as they begin]
[1 sentence: a specific detail about the work or place]
[Optional: a hint of the wider world continuing while the PC is
occupied]
```

**Example (researching in a city library):**

> The Old Library at Greycrest accepts you on the strength of the
> letter you carry. The Master Archivist, a woman with parchment-
> dry hands, leads you to a narrow study with no window. Stacks of
> bound volumes line all four walls; a half-finished cup of tea
> waits on a desk that hasn't been used in a season. Outside, the
> city's bell rings the noon hour — and you have a great deal of
> reading to do before sunset.

This entrance:
- Orients (Old Library at Greycrest, narrow study)
- Establishes the activity (research, working alone)
- Sets tone (academic, focused, solitary)
- Implies time pressure (noon bell, sunset deadline)

## 3. Resolution Read-Aloud — Patterns by Outcome

The encounter's resolution read-aloud captures *the moment after*.
Each major outcome gets its own short read-aloud (50–80 words).

### On primary victory

The PCs got what they came for. The read-aloud captures the
relief, the cost, and the next step.

**Template:**
```
[1 sentence: the moment of resolution — what changes]
[1 sentence: the cost or texture of victory]
[1 sentence: what now demands attention]
```

**Example (after defeating the cultists in the library):**

> The frost-eyed cultist crumples last, the ritual circle below her
> snapping into smoke. The chandelier — somehow still hanging —
> sways, then slowly settles. You stand alone in the reading room,
> the books at the circle's center charred at the edges. Above you,
> the windows show a sky that has shifted from grey to pre-dawn
> blue; the night is nearly over. The third floor is silent now,
> but it didn't feel silent five minutes ago.

### On alternative victory

The PCs solved the encounter without taking the primary route. The
read-aloud captures the unusual win.

**Example (cultists negotiated down rather than killed):**

> The frost-eyed cultist lowers her hands. The circle's light dims,
> not extinguished, but dormant. She studies you for a long moment,
> then says: "You've given us reason to wait. But not forever."
> The three of them gather their texts and walk past you toward the
> stairs. The circle remains, faint in the floor — a promise that
> the ritual can be resumed.

### On failure with survival

The PCs lost the encounter but lived. The read-aloud captures the
cost.

**Example (the party flees the library):**

> You retreat down the stairs, the cold pursuing as far as the
> ground floor before the cultists give up the chase. Outside, the
> sky is grey and indifferent. You're alive, but the books are
> gone, and so is whatever was in that ritual circle — whatever it
> was supposed to bring, it didn't reach completion, but neither
> did you stop it.

### On TPK or catastrophic failure (combat only)

Used rarely. Captures the moment of total loss.

**Example (TPK in the library):**

> The last of you falls, and the cold completes its work. The
> circle on the floor pulses once, then again, then settles into
> the dim violet of accomplished ritual. Whatever the cultists were
> calling has arrived. Somewhere in the city, the wind shifts;
> somewhere, a bell that should have rung does not.

## 4. Sensory Ladders — Building Atmosphere

A read-aloud is most effective when it engages multiple senses. The
"sensory ladder" is the order in which sensory details land most
naturally.

### Sensory priority order

1. **Sight** — primary, most automatic. Describe what dominates the
   visual field first.
2. **Hearing** — second. Sound that drifts in or stops.
3. **Smell** — third. Atmospheric, hard to ignore.
4. **Touch / temperature** — fourth. Often the moment of immersion.
5. **Taste** — last. Rarely useful unless intimate or specific.

### Pattern: the descending senses

```
[Sight — the dominant visual]
[Sound — what you can hear over the visual]
[Smell — what reaches you next]
[Touch — what you feel as you move]
```

Example:

> *Sight:* The cellar is lit by a single candle on the table. *Sound:*
> Water drips from a pipe in the far corner. *Smell:* The room
> smells of old wine and damp brick. *Touch:* The cold of the cellar
> floor reaches up through the soles of your boots.

### Pattern: ascending stakes

The read-aloud opens with the immediate and moves toward the
significant.

```
[Immediate sensory detail — what's in front of you]
[Wider scope — what's around you]
[Implication — what this place means]
```

Example:

> *Immediate:* A door of iron-banded oak stands ajar. *Wider:* Beyond
> it, a corridor sloping downward into darkness, with iron rings
> spaced every twenty feet — for what, you can't yet see. *Implication:*
> This is where they brought the prisoners.

## 5. Read-Aloud Pacing

Within a read-aloud paragraph:

### Short sentences for action and tension

> She bolts. The alley narrows. The crowd thickens. Behind you, the
> whistle.

### Medium sentences for description

> The reading room stretches the length of the western wing, three
> stories of bookshelves climbing toward a broken skylight that no
> one has repaired since the last storm.

### Long sentences for atmosphere and texture

> The cellar smells of old wine and damp brick and something else,
> something sweet that doesn't belong in a cellar at all — the
> faint, unmistakable trace of perfume, of jasmine, fading the way
> a memory fades when you try to hold it.

The variety of sentence length keeps the read-aloud rhythmic and
prevents it from feeling like a list.

## 6. Common Read-Aloud Pitfalls

### Pitfall: Over-Description

The read-aloud is a chapter of a novel. Players' attention drifts.

**Fix:** cap at 150 words for the entrance. Trim adverbs and
adjectives. Pick the *one* sensory detail that anchors each beat.

### Pitfall: Generic Sensory Detail

"It smells musty. It looks ancient. You feel uneasy."

**Fix:** specific sensory details. *What kind* of musty (wine cellar
musty? grave musty? damp wool musty?). *What kind* of ancient
(dwarven runes worn smooth? Roman-style mosaics? something older?).
Specificity is the engine of atmosphere.

### Pitfall: Premature Reveal

The read-aloud reveals information the PCs shouldn't know yet
(the hidden door, the secret passage, the disguised enemy).

**Fix:** describe only what would be perceived by a moderately
attentive PC without rolls. Use Perception / Investigation rolls
for the hidden.

### Pitfall: Telling the Feeling

"You feel afraid. You feel curious. You feel hopeful."

**Fix:** describe the *cause* of the feeling and let the players
feel it. "Your stomach tightens" is showing; "you feel afraid" is
telling.

### Pitfall: Forcing Player Action

"You draw your sword and step forward." / "You raise your hand to
the door."

**Fix:** describe the situation up to the moment of decision. End
with an invitation: "What do you do?" or simply by stopping mid-
description.

### Pitfall: Tone Mismatch

The encounter is grim, but the read-aloud is comedic. The encounter
is intimate, but the read-aloud is bombastic.

**Fix:** match the tone to the encounter's emotional register. A
fight against a beloved NPC turned villain is read-aloud-different
from a fight against a generic ogre.

## 7. Language Consistency

If the encounter is in French, the read-aloud is in French. The
voice should match the rest of the encounter document.

### French read-aloud patterns

French tends toward:
- Slightly longer sentences than English
- More vivid use of present tense for immediacy
- Sensory details slightly more abstract / atmospheric

**Example FR entrance:**

> Vous poussez les doubles portes de la grande salle. La lumière
> oblique d'un soleil déjà bas traverse les vitraux brisés trois
> étages au-dessus, et des poussières dansent dans les rayons. Trois
> silhouettes en robes sombres sont penchées sur un cercle de
> livres au sol, les mains levées au milieu d'une incantation.
> L'une d'elles se tourne ; ses yeux brillent d'une fine couche de
> givre. Un souffle froid roule depuis le cercle, embuant votre
> haleine. Au-dessus, un lustre pend de travers — à peine retenu.

Same beats, same length budget, same priorities — just in French.

## 8. Read-Aloud and Player Agency

Read-aloud sets the scene; it should never *deny the players' choices*.

### Phrasings to avoid

- "You enter the room." (the player hasn't chosen to)
- "You're scared." (the player decides what their character feels)
- "The figure in the doorway is your enemy." (the player decides if
  they recognize them)

### Phrasings to use

- "Before you stretches the room." (the room exists; the players
  decide to enter)
- "The figure stands in shadow, posture taut." (description; let
  the player interpret)
- "You see [X]." (factual sensory information; player decides
  meaning)

This is small but matters. Read-aloud that asserts what PCs do or
feel can feel intrusive even when subconscious.

## 9. Hand-off to read-aloud-crafter

This skill produces *functional* read-aloud that works at the table
but isn't necessarily publication-polished. For higher polish:

1. Generate the encounter with this skill
2. Hand off the read-aloud sections to `read-aloud-crafter`
3. `read-aloud-crafter` can apply specific tonal registers
   (literary, pulpy, epic, terse, mysterious, etc.) and rebuild the
   read-aloud at publication quality

Use this skill's read-aloud as the **first draft**; use `read-aloud-
crafter` for the publication draft.
