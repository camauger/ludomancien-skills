# Listing Copy Patterns

Title formulas, blurb structures, tag-stacking strategies, and the
specific patterns that convert browsers into buyers on DriveThruRPG.

This is the playbook for Axis 1 (Listing Copy) of `digital-product-evaluator`.

---

## 1. Title Formulas

A title has roughly **2 seconds** to convey what the product is in
the search grid. Eight words maximum, ideally five to six.

### Formula 1 — Hook + Category

`[Distinctive Hook]: A [Category] for [System]`

Examples:
- *The Lichgate Procession: A One-Shot for D&D 5e (2024)*
- *Saltwater Devotions: A Coastal Setting for OSR Games*
- *Tooth & Talon: A Bestiary for Cairn*

Why it works: the hook earns the click, the category resolves the
question "what kind of product is this", the system filters the
audience to qualified buyers.

### Formula 2 — Place + Genre

`[Place]: [Genre Descriptor]`

Examples:
- *Hollow Lantern Tavern: A Gothic Sanctuary*
- *Brassroot: A Cyberpunk Borough*
- *Salt March: A Drowned Frontier*

Why it works: the place anchors imagination, the genre tag immediately
qualifies the buyer.

### Formula 3 — Question Title

`[Specific Question with a Pull]?`

Examples:
- *Who Buried the Burgomaster?*
- *Why Does the River Drink?*
- *What Lurks Beneath Goldhaven?*

Why it works: a question creates an information gap the buyer wants
closed. Best for mysteries, investigations, and zines. Risky for
mechanical products (the buyer wonders if the product itself answers
the question or makes them work for it).

### Formula 4 — Number + Subject

`[N] [Specific Things] for [Purpose / System]`

Examples:
- *13 Cursed Roadside Shrines for Dark Fantasy*
- *50 Urban Encounters for D&D 5e*
- *7 Witches Who Refuse to Be Burned*

Why it works: a concrete count signals dense value and a finite
promise the buyer can evaluate against the price.

### Formula 5 — Persona / Antagonist Title

`[Name]: [Role / Threat]`

Examples:
- *Maren Voss: The Heretic Cardinal*
- *Yrsa Kett: Highwayman Saint*
- *The Pale Carpenter*

Why it works: villain supplements live or die by the character's
distinctiveness. The title sells the persona; the buyer wants to
know the rest.

### Anti-formulas (avoid)

**Fantasy noun-soup:**
- *Shadows of the Forgotten Crown of the Ancient Realm*
- *Whispers Beyond the Veiled Throne of Tears*
- *The Lost Chronicles of the Sundered Lands*

These titles convey *no specific promise*. The buyer scrolls past them
because the title could describe any of 200 products in the category.

**System-only:**
- *A D&D 5e Adventure*
- *PF2e Bestiary*

The title must do more than name the format and system.

**Tagline-as-title:**
- *Heroes Will Fall and Legends Will Rise*
- *In Darkness, A Light*

These work as **subtitles** but fail as titles — no concrete subject.

### Title length guidance

| Length | Effect |
|---|---|
| 1–3 words | Strong if the words are distinctive (works for persona / place titles); weak if generic. |
| 4–6 words | Optimal range. Room for hook + category. |
| 7–9 words | Maximum. Cuts off in mobile thumbnails. |
| 10+ words | Avoid; will truncate in browse and search results. |

### Subtitle usage

Subtitles appear under the main title on the listing. Use them when
the title can't carry both hook and category alone.

- **Title alone:** *Maren Voss*
- **Title + subtitle:** *Maren Voss · The Heretic Cardinal of Velnaris*

A subtitle that just restates the title is wasted real estate. A
subtitle that adds the genre / emotional / mechanical hook earns
its line.

## 2. Blurb Structure

The product description is the conversion engine. It must perform
three jobs in the first paragraph:

1. **What is it** (product type and premise)
2. **For whom** (system, level, audience)
3. **Why is it different** (the differentiator that justifies the buy)

If any of the three jobs is delayed past line 4, ~80% of skimmers
bounce.

### Structure template — Adventure / Module

```
[OPENING PARAGRAPH — 60–100 words]
[Premise in one sentence. Antagonist or stakes in one sentence.
What the players will face in one sentence. The one thing that
makes this adventure different from the 47 other 5e one-shots.]

[ATMOSPHERE PARAGRAPH — optional, 40–80 words]
[A taste of the tone, voice, or central image. This is where the
voice of the product shines. Cut if it doesn't earn the line.]

[WHAT'S INSIDE — bulleted, 4–8 bullets]
- [Page count, format]
- [Number of locations / scenes / encounters]
- [Notable mechanics, handouts, maps]
- [Pregens, NPC count, monster count]
- [VTT support / print-friendly version / extras]
- [Estimated session length]
- [System and level range]

[CLOSING HOOK — 30–60 words]
[The promise of what the GM will be able to do at their table.
Optional: a quote, a sample of read-aloud, a teaser of the twist
(without spoiling).]
```

### Structure template — Setting / Sourcebook

```
[OPENING PARAGRAPH]
[What the setting is in one sentence. The unique premise / genre
twist in one sentence. The kind of campaign it supports in one
sentence.]

[WORLD FLAVOR PARAGRAPH — 60–120 words]
[A vivid taste of the setting. Specific concrete details, not
abstractions. Names, places, sights, sounds. The buyer should leave
this paragraph able to picture the world.]

[WHAT'S INSIDE]
- [Total page count, color or B&W]
- [Number of regions / cities / factions detailed]
- [Number of NPCs, monsters, magic items]
- [Maps included (region, city, dungeon)]
- [System-specific content (stat blocks, mechanics, subsystems)]
- [Plot hooks count, adventure seeds]
- [Player-facing material vs GM-only]

[USE CASES — optional, 2–3 bullet scenarios]
- [How a GM might use this for a one-shot]
- [How a GM might use this for a campaign]
- [How a GM might fold it into an existing setting]

[CLOSING]
[The promise: what kind of stories this setting unlocks.]
```

### Structure template — Bestiary

```
[OPENING PARAGRAPH]
[Theme of the bestiary in one sentence. Number of creatures and CR
range in one sentence. The differentiator from generic monster
manuals in one sentence.]

[FEATURED CREATURE TEASER — 60–100 words]
[Describe one signature creature evocatively to demonstrate the
tone and quality of the writing. The buyer should think "I want to
throw this at my table."]

[WHAT'S INSIDE]
- [Creature count, CR range]
- [System and version]
- [Full stat blocks vs abbreviated]
- [Lore sections per creature?]
- [Variant rules, lair actions, regional effects (if applicable)]
- [Art: one piece per creature? Spot illustrations? B&W or color?]
- [Tactical guidance for GMs]

[CLOSING]
[The promise: a roster of monsters with personality, not just
math.]
```

### Structure template — Zine

```
[OPENING — voice-forward]
[The zine's premise, voice, and POV in one to two paragraphs.
Zines sell on voice and aesthetic. Lean into it.]

[WHAT'S INSIDE]
- [Page count, print format]
- [Sections or articles]
- [Art density and style]
- [System (or system-agnostic)]
- [POD availability]

[CLOSING]
[The promise: what kind of GM or reader this zine speaks to.
Specific, not generic.]
```

### Structure template — Toolkit / Generator

```
[OPENING PARAGRAPH]
[What the tool does in one sentence. What it solves in one sentence.
Why it's better than rolling on a generic table in one sentence.]

[USE EXAMPLE — 60–100 words]
[Walk through one specific example of using the tool. The buyer
sees the workflow concretely.]

[WHAT'S INSIDE]
- [Tables / generators count]
- [Page count]
- [Print-friendly / VTT compatible]
- [System-agnostic vs specific]
- [Cross-references / index]

[CLOSING]
[The promise: faster prep, more variety, less GM burnout.]
```

### Anti-patterns

**Wall of worldbuilding without product info:**

> In the ancient lands of Eldarinor, where the mists of forgotten ages
> drift across the sundered plains, three kingdoms stand on the brink
> of war. The Crowned Tyrant of the Veiled Throne has long...

→ The buyer is 200 words in and still doesn't know if this is an
adventure, a setting, a novel, or what system it's for.

**Marketing speak without specificity:**

> An exciting, immersive adventure that will challenge your players
> like never before! Filled with action, mystery, and unforgettable
> moments!

→ Every adjective is generic. Every claim is unverifiable.

**Spoilers in the blurb:**

> ...until they discover that the village priest is in fact the
> demon they were sent to slay, and the church bell rings with...

→ Cuts off half the buying audience who want to play and the other
half who would have bought to run.

**Blurb that doesn't list contents:**

A blurb without "What's Inside" leaves the buyer guessing about
length and density. Always include the bulleted feature list.

## 3. The "What's Inside" Discipline

The bulleted feature list lifts conversion measurably because it
answers the buyer's silent question: "what am I getting for my money?"

### Mandatory elements

- Page count
- System (if applicable) and version
- Format (PDF / softcover / hardcover availability)

### High-impact elements

- Number of NPCs / locations / encounters / monsters
- Maps (how many, what types)
- Handouts (count, content type: letters, journals, posters)
- Read-aloud text included
- VTT export (Roll20, Foundry)
- Print-friendly black-and-white version included
- Index / hyperlinked ToC

### Optional but useful

- Estimated session length (for adventures)
- Required reading time (for settings)
- Level range (for D&D-likes)
- Difficulty / GM experience required
- Player-safe vs GM-only sections

### Format

Use a tight bulleted list (5–10 bullets max). Long lists dilute. Bold
the *count* of each item for scannability:

```
- **64 pages**, full-color interior, print-friendly version included
- **17 NPCs** with full portraits and motivations
- **6 encounters** balanced for 4 PCs at level 3
- **3 maps** (village, manor, crypt) with numbered legends
- **8 handouts** ready to print or share digitally
- **VTT export** for Foundry VTT included
- **Read-aloud text** for every keyed location
- **Two endings** depending on player choices
```

## 4. Tag-Stacking Strategy

Every tag is a discovery surface. Stack tags from each applicable axis
(see `drivethrurpg-anatomy.md` Section 3 for the full taxonomy).

### Tag selection workflow

1. **System tag(s)** — primary system, plus any secondary system if
   the product converts trivially (a 5e adventure that includes OSR
   conversion notes earns both tags).
2. **Genre tag(s)** — usually 1 primary genre + 1 mood/sub-genre.
   E.g. "Fantasy" + "Dark Fantasy" + "Horror".
3. **Format tag** — Adventure, Setting, Bestiary, Toolkit, Zine, etc.
   This is non-negotiable.
4. **Content-warning tag** if applicable — Adult, Violence, Horror
   Themes.
5. **Sub-format / theme tags** — One-Shot, Campaign, Solo, GMless,
   PvP, Mystery, Investigation, etc.

### Tag count

- **3–5 tags:** under-tagged, missing discovery surfaces.
- **6–10 tags:** healthy. Each tag earned.
- **11–15 tags:** maximum useful. Diminishing returns above 10.
- **16+ tags:** spammy and may hurt the listing in DriveThruRPG's
  ranking signals.

### Common gaps

- Missing the **genre tag** when system tag is present → invisible
  to genre browsers.
- Missing the **format tag** → invisible to format browsers.
- Missing the **secondary system tag** for OSR-compatible products.
- Missing the **Adult tag** when needed → product gets removed.

## 5. Keyword Research

DriveThruRPG keywords feed internal search. The product's tags do not
fully cover this — keywords are *additional* search hooks.

### Research methods

- **Auto-complete:** start typing buyer queries in DriveThruRPG search
  ("one-shot for", "level 3 adventure", "horror for 5e") and note the
  auto-complete suggestions. These reflect real query patterns.
- **Competitor listings:** open the top 3 listings in your category.
  Note their keywords. Borrow the ones that fit.
- **Variant spellings:** "5e" / "5E" / "fifth edition" / "2024
  edition" / "DnD" / "D&D" / "D&D 5". Cover the spread.
- **Buyer vocabulary:** GMs search differently than designers. "Easy
  to run" / "one-shot" / "low prep" / "high prep" / "session zero" /
  "first-time DM" / "veteran DM" — match the buyer's language.

### Keyword count

- **5–10 keywords:** under-utilized.
- **10–20 keywords:** healthy.
- **20–30 keywords:** maximum useful.
- **30+:** diminishing returns and may signal keyword stuffing.

### Bilingual products (FR/EN)

A French-language product on DriveThruRPG should include:

- French keywords (the obvious ones)
- English equivalents (anglophones browsing for French content)
- The literal "French" or "français" keyword
- The system in both languages where relevant ("D&D 5e" works in both;
  "Donjons et Dragons" can be added)

## 6. Examples — Before and After

### Example 1: Generic title and weak blurb

**BEFORE**

Title: *The Shadow of Eldarinor*

Blurb:
> In the ancient lands of Eldarinor, three kingdoms stand on the brink
> of war. Heroes must rise to face the darkness that threatens to
> consume all. An exciting adventure for D&D 5e.

Tags: D&D 5e, Adventure

**AFTER**

Title: *The Lichgate Procession: A One-Shot for D&D 5e (2024)*

Blurb:
> Every seven years, the dead leave Eldarinor's cathedral in a slow
> procession through the city. This year, they're being herded. The
> party has one night to find out who's controlling the lich-led
> procession before it reaches the king's tomb at dawn.
>
> A four-hour mystery one-shot for 4–5 characters at level 5. Strong
> atmosphere, three clear leads, no railroad — the party can solve
> this in five different orders.
>
> **What's Inside:**
> - 24 pages, full color, print-friendly B&W version included
> - 6 keyed locations across the city at night
> - 11 NPCs with portraits and motivations
> - 3 maps (cathedral district, mausoleum, sewers)
> - 4 handouts (the heretic's letter, the procession route, a forged
>   prayer card, the king's tomb seal)
> - Pregens for instant convention play
> - VTT export for Foundry
> - Estimated session length: 3–4 hours

Tags: D&D 5e (2024), D&D 5e, Adventure, One-Shot, Mystery, Urban,
Horror, Fantasy, Dark Fantasy, Investigation

### Example 2: Setting product

**BEFORE**

Title: *Brassroot*

Blurb:
> Brassroot is a city. There are factions and politics. Use it in
> your campaign.

Tags: Setting, Fantasy

**AFTER**

Title: *Brassroot: A Cyberpunk Borough for D&D 5e and OSR*

Blurb:
> Brassroot is the borough nobody zones. Goblin techsmiths sell
> spell-grafts in alleys lit by harvested wisp-light. The guild towers
> next door pretend it doesn't exist. The party arrives at street
> level — what they leave with depends on how far they're willing to
> burn their hands.
>
> A drop-in urban setting compatible with D&D 5e (2024) and most OSR
> systems. Designed for 4–8 sessions of urban play but engineered to
> survive integration into any existing fantasy campaign.
>
> **What's Inside:**
> - 80 pages, full color
> - 5 factions with goals, methods, and inter-faction tensions
> - 23 keyed locations (shops, gangs, hideouts, sacred sites)
> - 17 NPCs with portraits, voices, and entanglements
> - 6 plot hooks ranging from heist to political coup
> - Random encounter table (d100, district-aware)
> - Two maps: borough overview and street-level grid
> - VTT-ready map exports

Tags: D&D 5e (2024), D&D 5e, OSR, Setting, Sourcebook, Urban,
Cyberpunk, Fantasy, Dark Fantasy, Borough, City, Campaign, Drop-in
