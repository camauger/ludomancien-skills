# Positioning Templates

USP frameworks, competitive landscape mapping, audience-channel-fit
analysis, brand alignment, and series positioning patterns.

This is the playbook for Axis 4 (Positioning) of `digital-product-evaluator`.

---

## 1. The USP Sentence Test

A product without a clear Unique Selling Proposition (USP) competes on
price and luck. A product with a sharp USP competes on identity.

### The single-breath sentence

The creator should be able to complete this sentence in one breath,
without hedging:

> *"This is the only TTRPG product that __________."*

The blank should contain:

- A **specific category position** ("the only 5e adventure that...")
- A **specific differentiator** (the thing this does that nothing
  else does)
- An **implied audience** (it's clear who this is for)

### Examples that work

- "This is the only 5e mystery one-shot designed so the players can
  solve it in five different orders without breaking the GM's prep."
- "This is the only OSR setting where the entire campaign frame is
  *being the city's funeral procession*, with mechanics for grief."
- "This is the only adult fantasy zine that pairs each illustration
  with an in-character monologue you can read aloud at the table."
- "This is the only francophone GM-advice fanzine written like a
  punk zine: hand-cut, opinionated, no consultants, no committee."

### Examples that fail

- "This is the only 5e adventure with great atmosphere." (Every
  competitor claims great atmosphere.)
- "This is the only OSR sourcebook for our world." (Tautology; the
  world is the creator's, of course no one else publishes for it.)
- "This is the highest quality adventure on DriveThruRPG." (Not
  falsifiable; not a positioning, a brag.)
- "This is for fans of dark fantasy." (Describes audience, not
  differentiator.)

### USP visibility test

Once the USP is clear, ask:

1. Is the USP visible in the **title** or **subtitle**?
2. Is the USP the **first sentence** of the blurb?
3. Could a buyer read **only the title and blurb** and recreate the
   USP sentence?

If any of these is "no", the listing is not surfacing the position.

### USP discovery for muddy products

When the creator can't complete the sentence, the issue is usually
one of:

- The product **does many things adequately** but has no
  differentiator → choose one to lean into.
- The differentiator is **about the world**, not about the product
  → ask "what does this product *do* that others don't?", not "what
  is this product *about* that others aren't?".
- The differentiator is **about the creator** ("written by someone
  who knows X") → that's an author bio, not a USP, unless the buyer
  cares about the credential.
- The differentiator is **the worldbuilding**, but the listing is
  for an adventure → the buyer wants adventure differentiation
  (structure, pacing, twists), not just worldbuilding.

## 2. Competitive Landscape Mapping

You can't position a product without knowing what it sits beside.

### Protocol

1. **Identify the niche.** What's the smallest category that
   accurately describes this product? Not "RPG product" — be
   specific. "5e level-3-to-5 one-shot urban mystery" is a niche.
   "OSR dark fantasy setting under 60 pages" is a niche.

2. **Find 5–10 direct competitors.** Search DriveThruRPG within that
   niche. Sort by Bestseller and by Recent. Pick:
   - 3 top-sellers in the niche
   - 2 recent releases in the niche
   - 1 "underdog" (low sales but interesting position)

3. **For each competitor, note:**
   - Title and subtitle
   - Price
   - Page count
   - First-line of the blurb
   - The differentiator (the thing they're claiming)
   - The cover style
   - The audience signal

4. **Plot the differentiation axes.** The 2–3 axes on which products
   in this niche differentiate. Examples:
   - Price (low → premium)
   - Length (zine → tome)
   - Tone (heroic → grimdark → horror)
   - Mechanical density (light → crunchy)
   - System (specific → agnostic)
   - Art style (B&W → full color → adult)

5. **Place this product on the axes.** Where does it sit relative
   to competitors? Where's the **empty space** (the position no
   one is occupying)?

### Outcome

The exercise produces:

- A **clear USP** anchored in real competitive space
- A **list of competitors to mention or differentiate against** in
  the blurb (without naming them by title; reference their pattern)
- An **awareness of price benchmarks** (which the pricing axis will
  use)

### When the niche is crowded

If the competitive landscape shows 50+ similar products and yours
isn't differentiated:

- Reframe the product to a **sub-niche** ("not just 5e one-shots,
  but 5e one-shots for **first-time DMs**")
- Lean into a **stylistic/tonal differentiator** ("not just dark
  fantasy, but *Mörk Borg-inspired* dark fantasy")
- Adjust the **format** to differentiate ("not 24 pages of prose,
  but a 24-page deck-of-cards adventure")

### When the niche is empty

If there are no comparable products, ask honestly:

- Is the niche empty because **nobody wants it**?
- Is the niche empty because **it's hard to make**?
- Is the niche empty because **you're early to it**?

The first is dangerous; the second and third are opportunities.

## 3. Audience Personas

The product is for a specific person, not "RPG players in general."
Sharper personas convert better.

### Common DriveThruRPG audience personas

Below are working sketches. Real audiences may overlap or fragment.

#### "The Busy 5e GM"

- 30–45 years old, employed, runs a weekly or biweekly group
- Looking for low-prep, drop-in adventures or NPCs
- Pays $5–15 routinely without flinching
- Trusts established creators and platform badges
- Skims blurbs; needs "What's Inside" bullets to convert
- Wants VTT support (Roll20, Foundry)
- Reads on Reddit r/DnD, r/DMAcademy, r/DnDBehindTheScreen

**Speak to them with:** practical promises, page counts, time
estimates, low-prep emphasis, system-version clarity (2014 vs
2024), VTT compatibility.

#### "The OSR Hobbyist"

- 25–50 years old, deep TTRPG history
- Reads more games than they run
- Values craft, voice, weird mechanics, evocative writing
- Pays $3–10 routinely for zines and small supplements
- Looks for distinctive voice in the blurb
- Cares about typography and layout craft
- Reads on Bluesky, ITO podcast network, Bones of Contention

**Speak to them with:** voice-forward prose, specific weird
details, system-agnostic-friendly framing, OSE/B/X/Cairn
compatibility callouts.

#### "The Solo Player"

- All ages; grew during the post-2020 solo TTRPG explosion
- Plays Ironsworn, Thousand Year Old Vampire, Wanderhome-likes
- Wants journaling prompts, oracle tables, self-contained adventures
- Pays $3–12 for clear, well-designed solo content
- Active on Bluesky, Itch.io, the Solo RPG Discord

**Speak to them with:** solo-friendly framing, journaling prompts,
oracle tables, no GM required, atmospheric prompts.

#### "The Adult Content Buyer"

- 25–55 years old, mixed gender, mixed orientation
- Often discreet about the hobby; relies on platform discretion
- Pays $5–25 for polished adult TTRPG content (higher than SFW
  equivalents)
- Values: production polish, content signaling (knowing what
  they're getting), creator trust
- Discovers via: existing creator follows, "Customers Also
  Bought" graph, Patreon

**Speak to them with:** clear content signaling, register that
matches the genre layer (dark fantasy / romance / horror /
sci-fi), production polish, consent and content warnings.

#### "The Francophone Hobbyist"

- 25–50 years old, France or Quebec primarily
- Reads English well but prefers French for play material
- Pays €5–20 for FR-language TTRPG content
- Loyal but smaller audience; community-driven
- Discovers via: SDEN, casus belli, Black Book Editions community,
  jeuxderole.org, FR TTRPG Discord, Casus NoLove podcast

**Speak to them with:** FR-language blurb, idiomatic FR (not
translated EN), references to FR play culture, FR-specific systems
or settings, FR + EN keywords for discovery.

#### "The Aspiring Designer"

- 20–35 years old, considering making their own TTRPG products
- Buys for inspiration / template purposes
- Pays $5–20 for craft-forward zines, design-forward products
- Values: explicit design choices, "designer notes" sections,
  unique formats
- Reads on Substack, Knock!, Design-focused podcasts

**Speak to them with:** explicit design choices, "designer notes"
sections, format innovation framing, transparency about what works
and what doesn't.

### Picking the persona

Most products have **one primary persona** and 1–2 secondary
personas. The listing should speak primarily to the primary, with
secondary cues that don't alienate the primary.

A product trying to speak to "all 6 personas at once" speaks to
none.

## 4. Audience-Channel Fit Matrix

Different platforms have different audience compositions. Where
the product is sold should match where the audience is.

| Platform | Primary audience | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| DriveThruRPG | D&D 5e and OSR hobbyists, broad TTRPG | Largest audience, internal discovery, POD, badges | Saturated, hard to break out, low margin for adult |
| Itch.io | Indie, narrative, system-agnostic, design-forward | Curation-friendly, supportive of weird formats, no NSFW gating | Smaller TTRPG-specific audience, less discovery infrastructure |
| Ko-fi | Creator's existing community | Direct relationship, recurring patron support | No discovery; you bring all the traffic |
| Patreon | Creator's existing community + ongoing access | Recurring revenue, deep audience relationship | Subscription fatigue, requires consistent output |
| Gumroad | Creator's existing community + email list | Simple checkout, flexible pricing | Minimal discovery; relies on creator's promotion |
| DMs Guild | D&D fans wanting Forgotten Realms / WotC IP | Legal protection for using WotC IP | Lower revenue split (50% vs 70%); IP-limited |
| Black Book Editions Store (FR) | Francophone TTRPG audience | French-first audience | Limited to BBE-friendly products; smaller reach |
| SDEN (FR community) | French TTRPG community | Trusted community channel | Community contributions, less commercial framing |

### Decision tree

- Product uses Forgotten Realms or WotC IP → **DMs Guild required**
- Product is mainstream D&D 5e or OSR → **DriveThruRPG primary**
- Product is system-agnostic / narrative / experimental → **itch.io
  primary + DriveThruRPG secondary**
- Creator has an existing audience that buys directly →
  **Ko-fi / Patreon / Gumroad primary** (no platform cut)
- Product is in French and FR audience is target → **DriveThruRPG**
  (FR keywords) **+ direct sales via Lulu / Patreon / personal site**

### Multi-platform strategy

Most established creators publish on 2–3 platforms:

- DriveThruRPG (broad TTRPG audience + POD)
- Itch.io (indie audience + weird formats welcome)
- Ko-fi / Patreon / direct (existing community, recurring revenue)

Cross-link between platforms in product pages where allowed.

## 5. Brand Alignment Audit

A product can be great in isolation and still hurt the brand if it
breaks the imprint's promise.

### What an imprint promises

An imprint is a **set of promises** the audience has internalized.
Open the imprint's last 5 products. What do they have in common?

- **Tone** (grimdark, comedic, literary, pulpy, sensual)
- **Format** (zines, full books, illustrated supplements, deck-based)
- **System scope** (5e-only, OSR-only, system-agnostic, multi-system)
- **Audience** (broad / niche, adult / SFW, mainstream / indie)
- **Voice** (terse / lyrical / academic / casual)
- **Production polish** (consistent layout, consistent typography,
  consistent illustration approach)

### When a new product breaks the brand

If a Ludomancien fanzine known for terse, hand-cut OSR weirdness
publishes a 200-page full-color D&D 5e mainstream campaign:

- The existing audience is confused ("is this for me?")
- The new product can't lean on imprint trust (it doesn't fit)
- The imprint dilutes ("what does Ludomancien stand for now?")

### Options when the new product breaks the brand

1. **Move it under a different imprint** — even a sub-imprint name
   ("Ludomancien Heavy" for the longer products).
2. **Frame the divergence explicitly** in the blurb — "you know us
   for X, this is the Y experiment."
3. **Rework the product to align** — pull it back toward the brand's
   identity.
4. **Accept the dilution** — sometimes the creator just wants to
   make this product and the brand cost is acceptable.

### Brand alignment for solo creators

Solo creators with 2–5 products are still defining their brand.
Each new product is a vote on what the brand will become. A solo
creator should:

- Decide: am I building a coherent imprint or a portfolio of
  varied work?
- If imprint: each product should reinforce the through-line.
- If portfolio: explicitly frame the brand as "creator name's
  work" rather than imprint identity.

## 6. Series Positioning

If the product is part of a series or catalog:

### Cross-promotion mechanics

- **In the blurb**: end with "Other titles in this series:" followed
  by linked product names.
- **In the product itself**: a back-page or final-page list of other
  available products with QR codes or short URLs.
- **In bundles**: package the series as a Bundle (see
  `pricing-benchmarks.md` Section 5).
- **In the "Also from this Publisher" graph**: DriveThruRPG surfaces
  this automatically; ensure all products carry the same publisher
  name to be linked.

### Reading order signaling

If the series has a recommended reading or play order:

- State it explicitly in each product's blurb
- Number the products in titles ("Vol. 1", "Book One", etc.) if
  order matters
- Or explicitly say "Can be read in any order" if order doesn't
  matter

### Series visual coherence

Series products should share visual identity:

- Same typography
- Same imprint logo placement
- Recognizable color palette (with variation per book)
- Series logo or banner element

### When to start a series

- If the first product earns Silver badge or higher, a follow-up
  in the same niche compounds audience.
- If the first product underperforms, **don't** double down with
  more in the same niche; diagnose the failure first
  (post-launch mode of `digital-product-evaluator`).

## 7. Positioning Worksheet

Use this to crystalize positioning before writing the listing copy.

```
PRODUCT POSITIONING WORKSHEET

1. The niche (smallest accurate category):
   ___________________________________________

2. The USP sentence:
   "This is the only TTRPG product that ___________________."

3. The audience persona (primary):
   Name: ________________________________________
   What they're searching for: ________________
   Where they look: ____________________________

4. Three competitors:
   - _________________________________________
   - _________________________________________
   - _________________________________________

5. Where this product sits vs competitors (the empty space):
   ___________________________________________

6. The promise to the buyer:
   "After buying this, you'll be able to ____________."

7. The brand alignment:
   Does this fit my imprint's identity? ___ Yes ___ No
   If no: ___ Reframe ___ Sub-imprint ___ Accept divergence

8. Series context:
   ___ Standalone ___ Part of series: ________________
```

A product that can't fill out this worksheet has a positioning
problem the listing copy can't solve on its own.
