# Player Personas

Five archetypes used in the simulated playthrough (pass 2) and per-persona
feedback (pass 3). Adapted from Robin Laws' player typology in *Robin's Laws
of Good Game Mastering*, sharpened for playtest use.

Each persona has:

- **Archetype** — the type, in shorthand
- **Priorities** — what they want from a session
- **Watching for** — what they'll flag as a problem
- **In-fiction behavior** — what they do at the table
- **Voice notes** — how they sound when they talk

Voice fidelity matters. If all five sound the same, the persona pass collapses
into one generic critique. Push for distinct cadence and concerns.

---

## Maël — The Tactician

**Archetype.** Strategic problem-solver. Treats the game as a series of
puzzles with consequences. Not the same as the Power Gamer: Maël cares about
*decisions mattering*, not about damage output.

**Priorities.**

- Stakes that are clear and consequential.
- Choices where different options lead to materially different outcomes.
- Rules applied consistently — if the DC for picking that lock was 15 last
  scene, it's 15 this scene.
- Time to think. A scene where the GM rushes the table past a meaningful
  decision is a bad scene.

**Watching for.**

- Illusory choice. Three doors that all lead to the same room.
- DCs pulled from thin air.
- "Make a check" without context for what success or failure means in the
  fiction.
- Combat encounters where the optimal opening trivializes the fight.
- Hidden information that the party had no way to find.

**In-fiction behavior.** Asks clarifying questions. Maps. Tracks resources.
Considers the second-order consequences of plans. Will say things like "if
we take this route, what do we know about what's between us and the target?"
Will sometimes propose alternative approaches the GM hadn't planned for.

**Voice notes.** Measured, precise, sometimes blunt. Uses out-of-character
phrasing more than the others — "I want to check the room before we move on."
Will name the mechanic when it matters: "I have advantage from Pack Tactics,
so."

## Salomé — The Method Actor

**Archetype.** Character-first roleplayer. Plays the fiction, not the
mechanics. Cares about who her character is and who the other characters in
the scene are.

**Priorities.**

- NPCs with interiority — agendas, contradictions, fears.
- Scenes that give time for dialogue and reaction.
- Moral ambiguity. Decisions that cost something.
- Continuity in characterization. The NPC who threatened them in scene 2
  should remember that in scene 5.

**Watching for.**

- NPCs who exist only to deliver information.
- Scenes that skip past the emotional beat to get to the next room.
- Read-aloud text that tells the party how their characters feel.
- Moral choices that have a clearly "right" answer.
- Combat that interrupts a scene she was invested in.

**In-fiction behavior.** Stays in character through transitions. Asks IC
questions and waits for IC answers. Will turn down treasure if her character
wouldn't take it. Sometimes derails the GM's planned scene by following a
thread the GM hadn't noticed.

**Voice notes.** Speaks in her character's voice unscripted-ly, even mid-
mechanics. Uses pronouns to signal character — "she would never..." or "I
walk away." Reflective, sometimes slow. Will pause before responding.

## Jonas — The Power Gamer

**Archetype.** Optimization-focused. Reads the rulebook. Builds the character
on paper before the campaign starts. Loves a calibrated, fair challenge.

**Priorities.**

- Encounters tuned to the party's actual capability.
- Build payoff — moments where his choices on the character sheet pay off in
  fiction.
- Mechanical clarity. He needs to know the AC, the save bonus, the resistance.
- Variety in challenge type. Five medium encounters is boring; one deadly +
  three easy is better.

**Watching for.**

- Trivially easy combats where his build does nothing meaningful.
- Save-or-suck effects with no counterplay against a single PC.
- Lethal damage spikes the party can't see coming.
- Encounters that ignore the party's level entirely.
- "Plot armor" — moments where mechanics get overridden for narrative reasons.

**In-fiction behavior.** Calculates damage in real time. Asks about target
ACs, saving throw modifiers, resistances. Coordinates with party members on
turn order optimization. Will sometimes call out a math error the GM made.

**Voice notes.** Mechanically literate, fast, transactional. "I cast hold
person, DC 16 wisdom save." Drops mechanical vocabulary without context.
Concise. When he does roleplay, it's usually one-line action description.

## Inès — The Storyteller

**Archetype.** Arc-focused. Wants a story with a beginning, middle, and end.
Cares about themes and resonance. Less about her character's interiority
(that's Salomé) and more about the shape of the whole.

**Priorities.**

- A clear narrative arc — stakes that escalate, a climax that lands.
- Themes that the scenario actually engages with.
- Callbacks. The thing introduced in scene 1 paying off in scene 7.
- Memorable beats. At least one image or moment she'll remember tomorrow.

**Watching for.**

- Episodic scenarios where nothing builds.
- A climax that's structurally a normal scene with louder music.
- Themes that the text claims but the play doesn't honor.
- Endings that fizzle — the BBEG dies and then there's twenty minutes of
  loot.
- "Worldbuilding for its own sake" — long lore dumps that don't connect to
  what the party is doing.

**In-fiction behavior.** Will sometimes propose narrative beats — "what if we
left the journal for the cult to find?" Tracks NPC names and relationships
across sessions. Asks the GM about themes after the session.

**Voice notes.** Reflective, sometimes meta. Will narrate her own actions in
prose: "She looks at the burning chapel for a long moment, then turns away
without speaking." Comfortable shifting between IC and OOC commentary.

## Tarek — The Specialist

**Archetype.** The niche-lover. Built his character around one or two things
he wants to be the best at. Investigation, magical knowledge, infiltration,
beast handling, whatever. Wants the scenario to let him use those things.

**Priorities.**

- Variety in pillar (combat / exploration / social). If it's all combat,
  half his sheet is dead weight.
- Spotlight moments — at least one scene per session where his niche is the
  key to forward progress.
- Opposition that engages his expertise. An investigation scenario for the
  investigator; a social-political tangle for the courtier.
- GM willingness to let his expertise *short-circuit* problems sometimes,
  not just adjudicate them.

**Watching for.**

- Scenarios that are mechanically one-pillar (all combat, all dialogue, all
  exploration). Even if well-built, they leave specialists cold.
- Skill checks where the party can roll-call until someone gets the number,
  making his +9 in the specialty redundant.
- "Critical clues" gated behind a single check at a single moment — if he
  rolls badly, the scenario stalls; if he rolls well, no one else mattered.
- Long stretches with no opportunity for niche expression.

**In-fiction behavior.** Hyper-attentive during scenes that match his
specialty, drifts in others. Will sometimes ask the GM if there's something
in the scene his expertise would notice. Brings up rules or lore the GM
might not have considered.

**Voice notes.** Knowledgeable, sometimes pedantic. Will cite source material:
"In Volo's Guide it says..." Specific. When his niche isn't relevant, he goes
quiet — and that silence is itself feedback.

## Coverage Heuristic

In the synthesis pass, run this check:

- Does **Maël** get at least one decision with meaningful branching consequences?
- Does **Salomé** get at least one NPC with an agenda and interiority?
- Does **Jonas** get at least one combat tuned to the party's actual capability?
- Does **Inès** get a clear arc and a landing climax?
- Does **Tarek** get a scene where his niche is genuinely useful?

If any of these is "no," that's a structural finding in the synthesis, not a
small note. A scenario that loses one archetype loses 20% of the table.

## Real Party Substitution

If the user provides a real party (names, archetypes, builds), substitute the
personas accordingly. Map roughly:

- Player who min-maxes → Jonas slot
- Player who writes character backstories → Salomé slot
- Player who plans the dungeon crawl → Maël slot
- Player who asks "what's this scene about" → Inès slot
- Player who picked an unusual class or focus → Tarek slot

The mapping is approximate. A real player is rarely a pure archetype. Use
your judgment; when in doubt, ask once.
